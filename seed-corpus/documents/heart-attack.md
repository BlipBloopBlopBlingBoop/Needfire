# Heart Attack and Chest Pain

A heart attack happens when blood flow to part of the heart muscle is blocked.
It can be dramatic or quiet, and it can slide into cardiac arrest, so treat
suspicious chest symptoms seriously and get help early.

## Warning signs
- **Chest discomfort** — pressure, squeezing, tightness, heaviness, or pain in
  the centre or left chest, lasting more than a few minutes or coming and going.
- **Pain spreading** to one or both arms, the jaw, neck, back, or upper stomach.
- **Shortness of breath**, with or without chest discomfort.
- **Cold sweat, nausea, light-headedness, or a sense of doom.**
- Women, older people, and those with diabetes more often have **subtle** signs:
  fatigue, breathlessness, nausea, or back/jaw ache without classic chest pain.

## What to do
1. **Get advanced medical help immediately.** Say "possible heart attack."
   Minutes of heart muscle are at stake.
2. **Stop all activity. Sit or half-sit and rest**, knees bent, back supported —
   this position eases the heart's load. Loosen tight clothing. Keep them calm.
3. **Aspirin:** if the person is not allergic to it and has no bleeding problem,
   have them **chew one adult aspirin (about 300 mg)** slowly. Chewing speeds it
   up; it helps stop the clot growing. (This is for suspected heart attack — not
   for stroke, where a bleed must be ruled out first.)
4. If they have their own prescribed **glyceryl trinitrate (nitroglycerin)
   spray/tablet**, help them take it as prescribed.
5. **Watch them constantly.** If they become unresponsive and are not breathing
   normally, **start CPR immediately** and use an AED if one is available. Early
   CPR is the single biggest factor in surviving cardiac arrest.

## Do not
- Do not let them "walk it off" or drive themselves.
- Do not give aspirin if they are allergic, actively bleeding, or if the picture
  looks more like a stroke.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
