# Wind Chill and Heat Index — What the Air Really Does to You

A thermometer tells you the air temperature; your body cares about how fast it
loses or gains heat. Wind and humidity move that line a long way, and both the
**wind chill** (cold + wind) and the **heat index** (heat + humidity) exist to
express the "feels-like" temperature that actually drives frostbite and heat
illness. The toolkit's exposure calculator uses the standard formulas below.

## Wind chill (cold + wind)
Moving air strips away the thin warm layer against your skin, so a windy 0 °C
freezes flesh far faster than a still 0 °C. The standard (US/Canada) formula, for
air at or below 10 °C and wind above about 5 km/h, is:

    WC(°C) = 13.12 + 0.6215·T − 11.37·V^0.16 + 0.3965·T·V^0.16

where **T** is air temperature in °C and **V** is wind speed in km/h at face
height. Below the calm value it only gets colder as wind rises.

**Frostbite time on exposed skin** falls sharply as wind chill drops:
- around **−28 °C** wind chill: frostbite possible in about **30 minutes**;
- around **−40 °C**: in about **10 minutes**;
- around **−48 °C** and below: in **5 minutes or less**.
Cover every patch of skin, add a windproof layer, and get out of the wind — a
shell that stops wind is worth more than bulk that does not.

## Heat index (heat + humidity)
Your body sheds heat by evaporating sweat; humid air stops that evaporation, so
a muggy 32 °C loads the body like a dry 40 °C. The heat index combines air
temperature with **relative humidity** to give the apparent temperature. Rough
danger bands (shade values; full sun adds up to ~8 °C):
- **27–32 °C**: caution — fatigue with exertion.
- **32–41 °C**: extreme caution — heat cramps and exhaustion likely.
- **41–54 °C**: danger — heat stroke likely with continued activity.
- **above 54 °C**: extreme danger — heat stroke imminent.

## Using it
- In the cold, plan around the **wind chill**, not the thermometer: it decides
  frostbite and hypothermia risk. See *Frostbite and Cold Injuries* and *Shelter
  and Hypothermia*.
- In the heat, plan around the **heat index**: rest, shade, and electrolytes as
  it climbs. See *Heat Illness*.
- Both are guides, not guarantees — wet clothing, exhaustion, age, and illness
  all shift real risk worse than the number.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained judgement. Conditions and
> individual tolerance vary; err toward caution.
