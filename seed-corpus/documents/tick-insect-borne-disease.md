# Ticks, Mosquitoes, and Insect-Borne Disease

Biting insects and ticks spread serious illnesses — Lyme disease, tick-borne
fevers, malaria, dengue, and more. In a disruption, prevention matters more than
ever because treatment may be far away.

## Prevention comes first
- **Cover skin:** long sleeves and trousers, trousers tucked into socks, light
  colours (ticks show up). A hat and closed shoes in tick country.
- **Repellent** on skin/clothing where available; permethrin-treated clothing
  repels ticks and mosquitoes.
- **Avoid peak biting times and places:** mosquitoes at dawn/dusk and near still
  water; ticks in long grass, brush, and leaf litter — walk the centre of trails.
- **Sleep protected:** a mosquito net (ideally treated) over sleeping areas;
  screen or smoke out biting insects; remove standing water where mosquitoes breed.
- **Check for ticks** at least daily — whole body, especially warm folds
  (groin, armpits, waistline, behind knees and ears, scalp). The sooner a tick is
  found and removed, the lower the disease risk.

## Removing a tick — the right way
1. Grasp the tick **as close to the skin as possible** with fine-tipped tweezers.
2. **Pull straight out with steady, even pressure.** Do not twist or jerk (the
   mouthparts can break off).
3. **Do NOT** burn it, smother it in oil/petroleum jelly, or crush its body —
   these make it regurgitate and raise infection risk.
4. Clean the bite and your hands. If mouthparts remain, remove them like a
   splinter or leave them to work out; the disease risk is from the body.
5. Note the date and the bite location.

## Warning signs after a bite — seek care
- **An expanding red rash**, classically a **"bull's-eye"**, days after a tick
  bite (Lyme disease). Treat early; do not wait for the rash if you have flu-like
  illness after tick exposure.
- **Fever, severe headache, body aches, chills, or rash** in the days to weeks
  after tick or mosquito bites — especially in malaria/dengue regions, where
  **fever is a medical emergency** and needs testing and treatment fast.
- Signs of a spreading skin infection at any bite: redness, swelling, warmth, pus.

## Note
Malaria and dengue are diagnosed and treated with specific drugs you likely do
not have in the field — prevention and getting to care are what save lives. Keep
any prescribed antimalarials taken exactly as directed.
