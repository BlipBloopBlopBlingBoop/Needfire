# Managing Pain and Fever

The common non-prescription painkillers also lower fever. Used correctly they are
safe and valuable; the dangers are **overdose** and using the wrong one for the
person. Always follow the actual package directions for the specific product.

## The everyday medicines
- **Paracetamol / acetaminophen** — good for pain and fever; gentle on the
  stomach; **safe in pregnancy** and the usual first choice for children (weight-
  based dosing). **Its danger is the liver:** overdose — even modestly over the
  daily limit, or combined with alcohol — causes delayed, serious liver damage.
  **Never exceed the stated maximum daily dose**, and watch for it hidden inside
  combined cold/flu remedies.
- **Ibuprofen (an NSAID)** — pain, fever, and **inflammation** (sprains, dental,
  period pain). Take **with food**. Avoid or use caution with stomach ulcers,
  kidney problems, dehydration, asthma (in some people), late pregnancy, and
  bleeding risk.
- **Aspirin** — pain/fever and, in a suspected heart attack, chewed to slow a
  clot (see *Heart Attack and Chest Pain*). **Never give aspirin to children or
  teenagers** (risk of Reye's syndrome), and avoid it where bleeding is a concern
  or a stroke may be a bleed.

## Dosing safely
- **Read the label** and dose by the product's instructions and the person's
  **weight** (especially children). Do not guess.
- **Respect the maximum daily dose and the minimum gap between doses.** More does
  not help faster and can harm.
- **Paracetamol and ibuprofen work by different routes**, so they can be
  alternated or combined for stronger relief when appropriate — but **never double
  up two products containing the same drug** (the commonest accidental overdose).
- Avoid alcohol with these medicines.

## Fever itself
Fever is a response to infection, not the disease. Treat the person, not just the
number: keep them **cool and hydrated** (sips of water or ORS), rest, and use
paracetamol/ibuprofen for comfort.
**Seek help** for: fever in a baby under 3 months, a stiff neck with headache and
rash, confusion or drowsiness, a fever that is very high or lasts several days,
trouble breathing, or dehydration.
