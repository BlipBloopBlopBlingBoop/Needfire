# Survival Priorities — The Rule of Threes

In an emergency the deadliest mistake is doing the wrong thing first. A simple
order of priorities keeps you working on what will actually kill you soonest,
instead of what feels urgent. Fix the fastest threat first.

## The Rule of Threes (rough survival time limits)
You can last roughly:
- **3 minutes** without **air** (or in icy water; or with severe bleeding).
- **3 hours** without **shelter** in harsh heat or cold (exposure kills fast).
- **3 days** without **water**.
- **3 weeks** without **food**.
These are approximate, but the **order** is the point: air and severe bleeding
now, then core temperature/shelter, then water, then food. Do not forage for food
while freezing, or purify a week's water while bleeding.

## STOP — before you move
When things go wrong, the first move is to **not** rush:
- **S — Stop.** Halt. Don't compound the problem with a panicked reaction.
- **T — Think.** What is the real, most-immediate threat? What do I have?
- **O — Observe.** Weather, terrain, injuries, resources, daylight left.
- **P — Plan.** Decide, then act deliberately. Re-assess as things change.
Panic and haste kill; calm, deliberate action saves. Controlled breathing steadies
the mind enough to think.

## Priorities of work (once immediate threats are handled)
A rough order for settling in:
1. **First aid** — treat injuries (see *Assessing a Casualty*).
2. **Shelter and warmth** — protection from wind, wet, heat, and cold (build
   before dark).
3. **Fire** — warmth, water purification, cooking, signalling, morale.
4. **Water** — find, then purify.
5. **Signalling** — make yourself findable if you want rescue (see *Signaling for
   Rescue*): three of anything means distress.
6. **Food** — last, and rarely urgent in the short term.

## The will to live and the group
- **Morale is a survival function.** Keep busy with useful work, set small goals,
  and protect the group's spirits — despair kills people the environment would not.
- **Stay put** if you are lost and expect to be searched for; a stationary,
  signalling group is far easier to find than a wandering one, unless staying is
  more dangerous than moving.
- **Look after the weakest** (children, injured, elderly) — the group's survival
  is measured by them.

> General reference only. Every situation differs — these priorities are a
> starting framework, not a substitute for judgement and training.
