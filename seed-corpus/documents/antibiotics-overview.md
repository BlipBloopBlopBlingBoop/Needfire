# Antibiotics — Principles and Penicillin

Antibiotics are among the most important medicines for surviving bacterial
infection. This is background on how they work and the history of penicillin
production — it is **not** a guide to self-treatment or unsupervised manufacture.

## How antibiotics work
Antibiotics kill or inhibit bacteria by attacking structures bacteria have but
human cells do not — for example the bacterial cell wall (penicillins) or
bacterial protein synthesis (tetracyclines, macrolides). They do **not** work
against viruses (colds, influenza, most sore throats).

## Resistance — the central danger
Misuse drives antibiotic resistance, which can render a drug useless. Principles:
use the right antibiotic for a confirmed bacterial infection, at the correct dose,
for the full course. Stopping early or under-dosing breeds resistant bacteria.
Resistance, once widespread, is extremely hard to reverse.

## Penicillin in history
Penicillin is produced by *Penicillium* mould. Alexander Fleming observed in 1928
that the mould inhibited bacteria. Turning that observation into a usable drug
required years of work by Florey, Chain, and others to grow the mould at scale,
extract and purify the active compound, and stabilize and dose it correctly.

The hard parts were never "growing mould" — they were **purification, potency
standardization, and sterility**. Crude mould extract is not a safe or reliable
medicine: dose is unknown, contaminants and allergens are present, and severe
allergic reactions (anaphylaxis) can kill.

## Practical takeaway
Preserve pharmaceutical knowledge and stockpile professionally made antibiotics
where lawful. Treat unsupervised production as a last-resort topic requiring real
microbiology, not a casual recipe.

> Educational reference only. Not medical advice. Antibiotic misuse is dangerous
> to the individual and to the community.
