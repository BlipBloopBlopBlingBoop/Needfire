# Burn Treatment

Stop the burning, cool the wound, cover it, and — for anything large — fight the
fluid loss that actually kills burn victims. Off-grid, a serious burn becomes a
wound you manage for weeks, so clean dressings and infection control matter as
much as the first aid.

## Stop the burning, then cool
- **Remove the person from the source.** Smother flames (stop, drop, roll). Cut
  the power before touching an electrical casualty.
- **Cool with cool running water for 20 minutes.** Not ice, not ice water — ice
  deepens the injury. Cooling still helps up to an hour after the burn. For a
  large burn, cool the wound but **keep the rest of the body warm** — cooling a
  big area causes dangerous hypothermia.
- **Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing early**, before swelling starts. Do
  not pull off clothing stuck to the burn.

## Cover it
Cover with a clean **non-stick dressing** or clean cling film laid loosely over
the wound (not wrapped tight). **Do NOT** pop blisters, or apply butter,
toothpaste, oil, grease, or ash — they trap heat and breed infection.

## How bad is it? (size and depth)
- **Size:** the victim's own **palm with fingers ≈ 1%** of their body surface.
  Count palms.
- **Depth:** **superficial** (red, painful, like sunburn) heals well;
  **partial-thickness** (blisters, wet, very painful) needs good wound care;
  **full-thickness** (painless, white/waxy, leathery, or charred) will need
  advanced care and often skin grafting — evacuate.

## When a burn is critical — evacuate
Treat as life-threatening if any apply: burns to the **face, hands, feet,
genitals**, or over joints; any **airway burn** sign (singed nostrils, hoarse
voice, soot in the mouth, burns around the mouth from a fire in an enclosed
space); more than **10% of body surface** (or ~5% in a child); **full-thickness**
burns; **circumferential** burns (all the way around a limb or the chest — swelling
can choke off circulation or breathing); or **smoke inhalation**.

## Fluid — the off-grid killer of large burns
A burn over roughly **10%+ of the body** (5% in a child) leaks huge amounts of
fluid and causes shock. With no IV, **give oral rehydration solution steadily** to
anyone awake and able to swallow (see *Oral Rehydration Salts*). Start early and
keep it up for the first day or two. **Aim to keep urine flowing and pale** — that
is your best field sign that fluids are keeping up. Add a pinch more salt than
usual; big burns lose salt.

## Ongoing wound care (days to weeks)
- **Keep it clean and covered.** Change the dressing when soaked or dirty; gently
  clean with clean (boiled, cooled) water.
- **Honey is a genuine field burn dressing** — spread a layer on a clean cloth
  over a partial-thickness burn; it fights infection and keeps the wound moist.
- **Watch for infection:** spreading redness, swelling, increasing pain, pus, bad
  smell, or fever. Treat and evacuate.
- **Elevate** a burned limb to reduce swelling; **gently move joints** through the
  day to prevent stiff, contracted scars.
- **Pain and nutrition:** give pain relief (paracetamol/ibuprofen); burns burn
  through calories and protein, so feed and hydrate well as they heal.
- Ensure **tetanus** protection is current.

## Special burns
- **Electrical:** damage runs deep between entry and exit wounds even when the
  skin looks minor; watch for heart-rhythm problems (start CPR if they collapse).
- **Chemical:** brush off dry powder first, then **flush with water for 20+
  minutes**; remove contaminated clothing; protect yourself. See *Eye Injuries*
  for chemical eye burns.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
