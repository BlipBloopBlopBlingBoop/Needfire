# Fractures and Splinting

Suspect a fracture when there is deformity, point tenderness over the bone,
inability to bear weight or use the limb, grating on movement, or rapid
swelling after an injury. When unsure, treat it as broken.

## Steps
1. **Do not move the person** more than necessary. Control any bleeding first.
2. **Check circulation, sensation, and movement** beyond the injury: feel for a
   pulse, ask about numbness, ask them to wiggle fingers or toes. Note what you
   find.
3. **Splint in the position found.** Use rigid material — a stick, board,
   rolled sleeping pad, trekking pole — padded with cloth so it never presses
   bare on skin or bone.
4. **Immobilize the joint above and below the break.** A forearm splint must
   stop the wrist and elbow from moving.
5. Tie the splint snugly above and below the injury, never directly over it.
6. **Re-check circulation, sensation, and movement** after splinting and
   regularly afterward. **If the pulse or feeling is lost, loosen the ties.**
7. Support a splinted arm in a **sling** against the chest. Elevate the limb
   to limit swelling.

## Open fractures (bone visible or wound over the break)
Cover with a **clean, moist dressing**. **Do not push bone ends back in and do
not scrub the wound.** Splint as it lies and evacuate — infection in bone is
grave.

## Do not straighten
Do not try to realign a limb **unless it has no pulse and help is many hours
away** — then apply gentle steady pull along its length to restore blood flow,
and splint.

## Thigh and pelvis — treat as serious
A broken **thigh bone (femur)** or **pelvis** can bleed a litre or more of blood
internally, with little to see outside. Treat these as potentially
life-threatening: keep the person still and flat, keep them warm, watch for
shock (pale, cold, fast heartbeat, confusion), and evacuate urgently. For a
mid-thigh femur fracture, steady traction — gentle, firm pull along the leg's
length — eases pain and bleeding if you are trained to apply it and help is far.

## Spine
If the injury could involve the neck or back (fall, blow to the head), **do not
move the person unless they are in immediate danger.** Hold the head still and
wait for help.
