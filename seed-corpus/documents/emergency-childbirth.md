# Emergency Childbirth

Most births proceed normally with little intervention. Your job is cleanliness,
warmth, and calm support — not to speed things up.

## Prepare
Wash your hands thoroughly and lay out clean cloths or plastic under the mother.
Let labor progress at its own pace; do not push on the abdomen or tell her to
push before she feels the urge.

## The birth
1. As the head crowns, support it gently with a clean hand so it emerges slowly.
   **Do not pull on the head or body.**
2. Check for the cord around the neck; if present, slip it gently over the head.
3. The shoulders and body usually follow within a contraction or two. The baby
   will be slippery — hold securely.

## Immediately after
- Dry the baby vigorously and place it **skin-to-skin on the mother's chest**,
  covered with dry cloth. Warmth is critical.
- Most babies cry and breathe within a minute. If not, rub the back firmly and
  flick the soles of the feet. **If the baby still does not breathe after about
  a minute of drying and stimulation, start gentle rescue breaths**: cover the
  baby's mouth and nose with your mouth and give small puffs (see the CPR
  document for infants). Do not give up quickly.
- **Do not cut the cord early.** Wait until it stops pulsing, then tie it tightly
  in two places with clean cord (about 5 and 10 cm from the baby) and cut between
  the ties with a clean blade.

## The placenta
It usually delivers within 30 minutes. **If it has not delivered within 30–60
minutes, treat it as a danger sign** — retained placenta causes severe
bleeding. **Never pull on the cord.** After it
delivers, massage the mother's lower abdomen firmly until the uterus feels like
a hard ball — this limits bleeding. Put the baby to the breast; nursing helps
the uterus contract.

## Danger signs — seek skilled help
Heavy bleeding, feet or cord appearing first, or labor lasting many hours
without progress are emergencies beyond home care.
