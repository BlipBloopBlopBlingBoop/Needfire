# Emergency Childbirth

Most births proceed normally with little intervention. Your job is **cleanliness,
warmth, and calm support — not to speed things up or pull.** The two things that
kill are the baby not breathing and the mother bleeding, and you can manage both.

## Prepare
Wash your hands thoroughly and lay clean cloths or plastic under the mother. In
**early labour** there is usually time to reach help — if help exists, start moving.
Let labour progress at its own pace; do not push on the belly or tell her to push
before she feels the urge. Keep her hydrated and calm.

## The birth
1. **As the head crowns, support it gently** with a clean hand so it emerges slowly
   — this protects the mother from tearing. **Do not pull** on the head or body.
2. **Feel for the cord around the neck** once the head is out; if it's looped, slip
   it gently over the head. If it's too tight, deliver the baby quickly.
3. **Deliver the shoulders** with the next contraction: guide the head **gently
   down** to free the top shoulder, then **gently up** for the lower. The body
   follows fast and slippery — hold securely.

### If it doesn't go smoothly
- **Shoulders stuck after the head is out (shoulder dystocia):** get the mother's
  **knees up to her chest** (McRoberts position) and have someone press just above
  the pubic bone. Do **not** pull hard on the head.
- **Baby coming bottom- or feet-first (breech):** let it deliver on its own,
  supporting the body without pulling; keep the body warm and wait for the head. If
  the head delays, lift the body slightly and clear the baby's mouth. Breech and a
  cord/limb appearing first are reasons to reach skilled help if at all possible.

## The newborn — dry, warm, breathing
Dry the baby **vigorously** and place it **skin-to-skin on the mother's bare
chest**, covering both (especially the head). Warmth and drying are usually all it
takes to start breathing. Note the time of birth.
- **If not breathing after ~1 minute** of drying and rubbing: wipe the mouth and
  nose, then give **gentle puffs of air** — just enough to lift the chest, about
  30–40 per minute.
- **Still no breathing or heartbeat** after good inflation breaths: start infant
  CPR at **3 compressions to 1 breath** (two thumbs on the breastbone). **Keep the
  baby warm throughout** — cold babies don't respond. See *CPR for Adults,
  Children, and Infants*.

## The cord
**Do not cut it early.** Leave it until it **stops pulsing** (several minutes) so
the baby receives its full blood. Then tie it tightly in two places (about 5 and 10
cm from the baby) with clean cord and cut between the ties with a **clean,
flame-sterilised** blade.

## The placenta and stopping the bleeding
The placenta usually delivers within **5–30 minutes**. Let it come with a gentle
maternal push — **never pull the cord** (it can turn the womb inside out). Keep the
placenta and check it looks complete.

**Bleeding after birth is the biggest killer of mothers.** As soon as the placenta
is out, **massage the womb**: rub the lower belly firmly until it hardens into a
ball, and **put the baby to the breast** — both clamp the womb down. If bleeding is
heavy, keep the womb massaged firm, lay her flat with legs raised, keep her warm,
give fluids by mouth if fully alert, and **evacuate urgently** (this is when
misoprostol or oxytocin is given, if you have it and know how).

## Danger signs — get skilled help / evacuate
Heavy bleeding; a **foot, arm, or cord appearing before the head**; the placenta
not delivering within ~30–60 minutes; the baby not breathing well; or the mother
becoming faint, feverish, or very unwell. Keep mother and baby **warm, together,
and monitored**.
