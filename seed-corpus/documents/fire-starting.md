# Starting a Fire

Fire provides warmth, water purification (boiling), cooking, light, signalling,
and morale. Prepare materials before you make a spark.

## The three fuel stages
1. **Tinder** — catches a spark and flames easily: dry grass, birch bark, char
   cloth, cotton lint, fine wood shavings, pine resin.
2. **Kindling** — small dry twigs from pencil-thick down to matchstick-thin.
3. **Fuel wood** — progressively larger dry branches and split logs.

Have all three gathered and sorted by size before lighting. Dead, dry, off-the-
ground wood burns best; standing dead branches stay drier than ground wood.

## Building it
Make a small structure that lets air reach the flame: a **teepee** (cone of
kindling around tinder) or a **lean-to** against a larger log. Light the tinder,
shield it from wind, and feed kindling gradually. Do not smother a young flame
with large wood.

## Ignition sources
Matches, lighter, ferrocerium rod (scrape sparks into tinder), magnifying lens in
strong sun, or a bow drill for friction fire (difficult; practice first).

## Safety
Clear a circle of bare earth or stone around the fire; keep water or soil nearby
to extinguish it. Never leave a fire unattended. Beware carbon monoxide — never
burn fuel in an unventilated enclosed shelter.
