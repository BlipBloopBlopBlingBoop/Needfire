# Asthma Attack and Sudden Breathing Trouble

In an asthma attack the airways narrow and clog, so breathing out becomes hard
and slow. It ranges from mild wheeze to a life-threatening emergency. The
mainstay of treatment is the person's own **reliever inhaler** (usually blue —
salbutamol/albuterol) used correctly and repeatedly.

## Recognising an attack
- **Wheezing** (a whistling sound, mostly breathing out), coughing, tight chest,
  and struggling to breathe.
- Speaking in short phrases or single words, not full sentences.

**Danger signs — treat as a severe emergency and get help now:**
too breathless to speak or eat, lips or fingertips turning **blue/grey**,
exhaustion, a silent chest (wheeze stops because too little air moves),
drowsiness or confusion, or a reliever that is not helping.

## What to do
1. **Sit them upright** — do not let them lie down. Leaning slightly forward with
   arms supported helps. Keep them calm; panic tightens breathing.
2. **Help them use their reliever inhaler**: one puff at a time, ideally through
   a spacer, breathing it in slowly. Typically **up to 10 puffs**, one every
   30–60 seconds.
3. If it does not ease within a few minutes, or any danger sign is present,
   **get emergency help** and keep repeating the reliever (up to 10 puffs) every
   10–15 minutes while you wait.
4. Loosen tight clothing and move away from an obvious trigger (smoke, cold wind,
   animal, pollen) if you can do so without delay.

## If breathing stops
If the person becomes unresponsive and is not breathing normally, **start CPR.**

## Without an inhaler
There is no good field substitute. Keep them calm, upright, and in fresh, humid,
smoke-free air; slow, pursed-lip breathing (in through the nose, out slowly
through pursed lips) can help a mild attack. Warm, moist air may ease it. Get to
medical help — a severe attack needs drugs you do not have.

> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
