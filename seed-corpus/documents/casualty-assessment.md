# Assessing a Casualty — DR-ABC and the Recovery Position

Whatever the injury or illness, first aid follows the same first minute: make it
safe, wake them, open the airway, check breathing, and fix immediate killers in
order. This is the **primary survey**, remembered as **DR-ABC**. Work top to
bottom and do not move on until each step is handled.

## DR-ABC — the primary survey
- **D — Danger.** Check the scene for hazards before you approach: traffic,
  fire, live wires, gas, water, unstable ground, an aggressive animal. **Do not
  become a second casualty.** Remove the danger or the person if it is safe.
- **R — Response.** Speak loudly and gently squeeze the shoulders: "Can you hear
  me? Open your eyes." Grade it with **AVPU** (see below).
- **A — Airway.** If unresponsive, open the airway: tilt the head back and lift
  the chin. Look in the mouth and clear obvious blockage.
- **B — Breathing.** Look, listen, and feel for **normal** breathing for up to
  10 seconds. Occasional gasps are NOT normal breathing.
  - **Breathing normally** → put them in the recovery position and monitor.
  - **Not breathing normally** → this is cardiac arrest: **start CPR now** and
    send for help / an AED.
- **C — Circulation.** Once breathing is handled, find and stop **severe
  bleeding** with firm direct pressure, and watch for signs of shock.

Only after DR-ABC is stable do you do a head-to-toe **secondary survey** for
other injuries.

## AVPU — a fast level-of-response scale
- **A — Alert:** eyes open, aware, talking.
- **V — Voice:** responds only when you speak to them.
- **P — Pain:** responds only to a firm pinch.
- **U — Unresponsive:** no response at all.
Anything below **A** is serious; a falling level over time is an emergency.

## The recovery position
For an **unresponsive person who IS breathing normally** (and has no suspected
spinal injury you can avoid moving):
1. Kneel beside them; straighten their legs.
2. Place the near arm out at a right angle, elbow bent, palm up.
3. Bring the far arm across the chest, back of the hand against their near cheek.
4. Bend the far knee up; using it as a lever, roll them toward you onto their
   side.
5. Tilt the head back so the airway stays open and any fluid drains out. Check
   breathing continuously.

If a spinal injury is likely but they cannot protect their own airway, an open
airway still wins — keep the head and neck as aligned as you can while you turn
them.
