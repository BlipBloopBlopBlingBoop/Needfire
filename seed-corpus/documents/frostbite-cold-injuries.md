# Frostbite and Cold Injuries

Cold injures in stages. **Frostnip**: skin numb, white, but **still soft** —
rewarm it skin-to-skin (fingers in an armpit, a warm palm on a cheek) and no
harm is done. **Frostbite**: tissue **hard, waxy, wooden** — the tissue is
frozen and how you rewarm it decides how much survives.

## First moves
Get out of the cold. Remove wet clothing, and remove **rings and watches**
before swelling. Protect the frozen part from bumps and pressure.

## The refreezing rule
**Do NOT rewarm frozen tissue if there is any chance it will refreeze** —
refreezing destroys tissue far worse than staying frozen a few hours longer.
**Walking on frozen feet to reach shelter is acceptable; walking on thawed
feet is not.** Choose the moment of rewarming deliberately.

## Rewarming
Once refreezing is impossible:
1. Immerse the part in **warm water at 37–39°C (99–102°F)** — comfortable to
   your own elbow, never hot.
2. Keep it there **20–30 minutes**, topping up warm water, until the tissue is
   soft and red or purple.
3. **Severe pain during rewarming is expected** and is a sign of returning
   circulation. Give pain relief if available.

**NEVER rub the part with snow, massage it, or hold it to direct dry heat** —
a fire or stove will burn tissue that cannot feel. **Leave blisters intact.**
Afterward, place **dry gauze between fingers and toes**, bandage loosely,
elevate, and do not let the part refreeze.

## Trench foot (immersion foot)
Wet and cold **above** freezing for hours to days: feet numb, pale, wrinkled,
then painful and swollen. Treat with **dry socks, air the feet**, keep them
clean, elevate, and rewarm **gradually** — not in hot water. Prevent it by
changing into dry socks daily.
