# Frostbite and Cold Injuries

Cold injures in stages, and how you rewarm a frozen part decides how much of it
survives. Always check the **whole person** first — someone with frostbite is
often becoming **hypothermic**, which is the greater threat (see *Shelter and
Hypothermia*). Warm the core before you fuss over fingers.

## Know the stage
- **Frostnip:** skin numb, white, but **still soft**. Rewarm skin-to-skin (fingers
  in an armpit, a warm palm on a cheek) — no lasting harm.
- **Superficial frostbite:** skin white/waxy and firm on top but soft underneath;
  rewarms with clear blisters.
- **Deep frostbite:** the part is **hard, waxy, wooden**, cold, and dead-white or
  bluish — frozen through. This needs careful rewarming and, later, real care.

## First moves
Get out of the cold and wind. Remove wet clothing and take off **rings and
watches** before swelling. Protect the frozen part from knocks and pressure. **Do
not rub it** — ice crystals in the tissue tear it from the inside.

## The refreezing rule — decide before you thaw
**Do NOT rewarm frozen tissue if there is any chance it will refreeze.**
Refreezing destroys tissue far worse than staying frozen a few hours longer.
**Walking out on frozen feet to reach shelter is acceptable; walking on thawed
feet is not.** Choose the moment of rewarming deliberately, where it can stay warm.

## Rewarming (once refreezing is impossible)
1. Immerse the part in **warm water at 37–39 °C (99–102 °F)** — comfortable to your
   own elbow, never hot.
2. Keep it there **20–40 minutes**, topping up warm water, until the tissue is
   **soft and flushed red or purple**.
3. **Severe pain during rewarming is expected** and means circulation is returning
   — give pain relief (paracetamol/ibuprofen) if you have it.

**Never** rub with snow, massage the part, or hold it to a fire, stove, or hot rock
— numb skin burns without feeling it. **Leave blisters intact.** Afterward, place
**dry gauze between fingers and toes**, bandage loosely, elevate, keep it clean,
and **do not walk on a thawed foot**. Watch for infection over the days that
follow. The true depth of damage takes weeks to declare — **evacuate for care.**

## Trench foot (immersion foot)
Wet and cold **above** freezing for hours to days: feet numb, pale, wrinkled, then
red, painful, and swollen. Treat by getting the feet **dry and warm gradually**,
airing them, keeping them clean and elevated — **not** in hot water, and do not
rub or walk on them. Prevent it by keeping feet dry and changing into dry socks
daily; it disables people who never freeze.

## Prevention
Cover every bit of skin in wind and cold, keep dry, keep moving to stay warm, and
watch each other's faces for white patches. Plan around the **wind chill**, not the
thermometer — see *Wind Chill and Heat Index*.
