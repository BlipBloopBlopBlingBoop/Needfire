# Sanitation and Latrines

In disasters, sanitation prevents more deaths than medicine. Nearly all camp
epidemics travel the same road: feces to hands, flies, water, or food, and back
to mouths. Break that road.

## Siting a pit latrine
- **At least 30 m (100 ft) from any water source — well, stream, pond — and
  downhill from it.**
- At least 15 m from dwellings and kitchens, downwind if possible.
- Pit bottom at least 1.5 m above the water table; do not dig into ground that
  floods.

For a solo traveler or short stays, dig a **cathole** 15–20 cm deep, well away
from water and trails, and fill it in after use.

## Using and maintaining it
- **Cover waste with a scoop of soil, ash, or lime after every use.** This
  controls flies and odor — flies are the disease highway between latrine and
  kitchen.
- Keep a lid over the hole. Close and re-site the latrine when filled to within
  half a metre of the surface.

## Handwashing — at the latrine
Build a handwashing station **at the latrine itself**: a tippy-tap (hung jug
tipped by a foot-operated stick and string) with soap, or clean ash if soap is
gone. **Never handle food after using the latrine without washing.**

## Other waste streams
Greywater (washing water) can soak away in a gravel pit away from water
sources; blackwater (anything fecal) belongs only in the latrine. Burn or
deep-bury diapers and menstrual waste, and wash reusables in hot water; give
users privacy and supplies.

## When someone has diarrhea
Isolate their waste strictly — a dedicated latrine or bucket if at all
possible, extra cover material, and rigorous handwashing by them and any
caregiver.
