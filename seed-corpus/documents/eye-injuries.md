# Eye Injuries and Chemical Splashes

Eyes are fragile and heal poorly once scarred, so the priorities are to flush
out what can be flushed, protect what cannot, and never make it worse by
rubbing or pressing.

## Chemical splash — flush immediately, this is the emergency
Acids, alkalis (lye, cement, bleach, ammonia), and other chemicals damage the
eye by the second. **Alkalis are the worst — they keep burning long after
contact.**
1. **Flush with clean water at once** — do not hunt for an antidote or the
   "right" solution first. Any clean, drinkable water is right.
2. **Flush continuously for at least 15–20 minutes** (longer for alkalis). Hold
   the lids open; pour from the inner corner outward so runoff does not cross
   the other eye. Let the person tilt the affected eye down and outward.
3. Remove contact lenses after you start flushing, if present.
4. Keep flushing on the way to help. Bring the chemical's name or container.

## Something in the eye (foreign body)
1. **Do not rub.** Blink, and let tears wash it toward the corner.
2. If you see a loose particle on the white or inner lid, lift it off gently with
   a clean, damp cloth corner or flush it out with clean water.
3. A speck under the upper lid can sometimes be freed by pulling the upper lid
   out and down over the lower lashes.
4. **If it is stuck, on the coloured part (iris/pupil), or will not come out
   with gentle flushing, stop** — cover the eye and seek help. Forcing it
   scratches the cornea.

## Embedded or penetrating object — do NOT remove it
A fishhook, splinter, metal shard, or anything driven into the eye:
- **Leave it in place.** Removing it can empty the eye.
- Do not press on the eye. Steady the object if long; a paper cup taped over the
  eye (not touching the object) shields it.
- **Cover BOTH eyes** loosely — the eyes move together, so bandaging only the
  hurt one still lets it move. Explain this so the person is not frightened by
  the dark.
- Keep them calm, still, and upright, and get advanced care urgently.

## Blows and other injuries
For a black eye or blunt hit, apply a cold pack gently (no pressure) and watch
for warning signs needing urgent care: vision loss or blurring, blood pooling
over the iris, an irregular pupil, or something protruding.
