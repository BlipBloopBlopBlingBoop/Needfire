# Radio Communications Basics

When phones and internet fail, radio still carries voice and information. You do
not need to transmit to benefit — **a receiver keeps you informed**; a transceiver
lets you coordinate. Know your local licensing rules (transmitting on many bands
requires a licence except in a genuine emergency).

## Listen first — receivers
- A **battery/solar/hand-crank AM-FM-shortwave radio** picks up emergency
  broadcasts, weather, and news. In many regions a **weather-radio band (NOAA)**
  carries continuous alerts.
- **Shortwave (HF)** reaches across countries and continents — valuable when local
  stations are down.
- **A scanner** or wideband receiver lets you monitor local services and other
  radios.

## Bands — reach vs. simplicity
- **HF (shortwave, ~3–30 MHz):** long range, bouncing off the ionosphere — regional
  to worldwide. Needs more skill, bigger antennas, and (to transmit) a licence.
- **VHF/UHF (~30 MHz–3 GHz):** mostly **line-of-sight** — good for local, a few km
  to tens of km, more with height or a repeater. This is handheld "walkie-talkie"
  territory (licence-free family bands like PMR/FRS; licensed GMRS/amateur for more
  power and range).
- **Line-of-sight loves height:** range grows hugely from a hilltop or with a
  raised antenna; radios in valleys struggle.

## Antennas — the biggest free upgrade
- **Height and a clear path matter more than power.** Get the antenna up and in
  the open.
- Keep the antenna **vertical** to match other stations (polarisation), and keep
  the whip full length — a folded or damaged antenna cripples range.
- A simple **half-wave dipole** cut to the band (a wire on each side, each about a
  quarter-wavelength) strung between trees vastly outperforms a rubber "stubby"
  antenna. Wavelength (m) ≈ 300 ÷ frequency (MHz).

## A simple comms plan
Agree in advance: **primary and backup frequencies/channels**, **times to listen**
(e.g. on the hour), **call signs/names**, and short **brevity codes** for common
messages. Keep transmissions short to save battery and air time. Carry a written
copy — do not rely on memory.

## Power and safety
Radios sip power on receive and gulp it on transmit — budget batteries and keep
solar/crank charging available. **Do not transmit into a disconnected or missing
antenna** (it can damage the radio), and keep away from transmitting antennas at
high power. In a life-threatening emergency you may use any means to call for help
regardless of licensing.

## Safety
Follow local radio regulations in normal times. Store spare batteries charged and
protected; label your radios and keep the comms plan with them.
