# Fishing and Trapping Basics

For calories per unit of effort, fishing and trapping beat hunting. The
principle is **passive over active**: set many devices that work while you rest,
and check them on a schedule.

## Improvised fishing
- **Gorge hook**: a short sliver of wood or bone, sharpened at both ends,
  tied at its middle and buried in bait. The fish swallows it and it lodges
  crosswise. Simple and effective when hooks are gone.
- Line: the inner strands of paracord serve well; braid for strength.
- Bait with what fish already eat there — insects, worms, scraps of meat.
- Set multiple baited lines overnight, tied to flexible branches, and run them
  morning and evening.

## Fish traps
In shallows, build a **funnel weir**: stakes or stones forming a V or heart
shape with a narrow mouth. Fish swim in easily and rarely find the way out.
Herd fish toward it or let the current do it.

## Snares for small game
Set a wire or cord loop over a rabbit run or game trail, about a fist above the
ground, anchored firmly — an "engine" (bent sapling) that lifts the catch kills
faster and keeps it from predators. Set many; expect a low hit rate. A
**deadfall** (heavy rock or log on a trigger) works for small animals along
runs. **In any non-emergency, check local law first — traps and snares are
regulated or prohibited in most places.**

## Where and when
Animals move along edges — shorelines, field margins, trail junctions — and
near water, mostly at dawn and dusk. Set devices there. On ice, extreme
caution: test thickness before venturing out.

## Ethics and economy
Check traps at least daily and dispatch caught animals quickly. Do not set more
than you can check. Waste nothing.
