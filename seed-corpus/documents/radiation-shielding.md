# Radiation Basics and Shielding

Understanding ionizing radiation matters for safety after nuclear accidents,
fallout, or when working near radioactive sources. This is safety and physics
reference, not weapons information.

## Types of ionizing radiation
- **Alpha** particles: stopped by paper or skin. Dangerous mainly if the source
  is inhaled or ingested (internal exposure).
- **Beta** particles: stopped by a few millimetres of plastic, glass, or aluminium.
- **Gamma** rays and **X-rays**: highly penetrating; need dense material to attenuate.
- **Neutrons**: penetrating; best shielded by hydrogen-rich materials (water,
  polyethylene, concrete).

## The three protections
1. **Time** — minimize time near a source; dose accumulates with exposure time.
2. **Distance** — dose falls off with the square of distance (inverse-square law):
   double the distance, roughly one-quarter the dose.
3. **Shielding** — put mass between you and the source.

## Shielding for gamma
Effectiveness scales with material density and thickness. Lead is excellent per
centimetre; but thick **concrete, packed earth, or water** also work and are far
more available. A common fallout-shelter guideline is that each "halving
thickness" of a material cuts gamma dose in half — for packed earth this is
roughly 9 cm (about 6 cm of concrete, 1 cm of lead). Stack enough mass (earth, sandbags, concrete,
water containers, books) between you and fallout, and stay low and central.

## Fallout practicality
After fallout, shelter with maximum mass overhead and around you, seal against
dust, and wait. Radiation from fresh fallout drops quickly over hours and days
(the "7:10 rule of thumb": roughly tenfold decrease for each sevenfold increase in
time). Avoid tracking contaminated dust inside; remove outer clothing before
entering the shelter space.
