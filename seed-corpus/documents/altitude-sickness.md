# Altitude Sickness — AMS, HACE, and HAPE

Above roughly **2,500 m (8,000 ft)** the thin air can make people ill, and going
up too fast is the cause. Most cases are mild, but two forms — brain swelling
(HACE) and lung fluid (HAPE) — kill. **The universal cure is to go down.**

## Acute mountain sickness (AMS) — common, mild
Hours after arriving high: **headache** plus any of nausea, loss of appetite,
fatigue, dizziness, or poor sleep. It feels like a hangover.
- **Stop ascending.** Rest, hydrate, and wait for it to settle — usually a day or
  two as you acclimatise.
- Simple pain relief helps the headache. **Do not climb higher until symptoms
  are gone.**
- If it worsens instead of easing, **descend.**

## HACE — high-altitude cerebral oedema (brain)
AMS that progresses to the brain: **confusion, clumsiness, a staggering "drunk"
walk (ataxia), drowsiness,** behaviour change. This is life-threatening.
- **Descend immediately**, as far as possible (at least 500–1,000 m), with the
  person assisted — do not let them go alone. Descent cannot wait for morning.

## HAPE — high-altitude pulmonary oedema (lungs)
Fluid in the lungs, sometimes without much AMS first: **breathlessness at rest,**
a dry then wet cough, chest tightness, gurgling breaths, very poor exertion
tolerance, blue lips. Also life-threatening.
- **Descend immediately** and keep the person warm and as calm and rested as
  possible; exertion makes it worse.

## Prevention — the golden rules
- **Climb high, sleep low.** Above 3,000 m, raise your **sleeping** altitude by
  no more than ~300–500 m per night, with a rest day every ~1,000 m.
- Drink enough, avoid alcohol and heavy exertion on arrival, and never ascend
  with unresolved symptoms.
- **Any brain or lung sign (ataxia or breathlessness at rest) means go down NOW.**

Note: water boils at a lower temperature up high — boil for **3 minutes** above
~2,000 m to disinfect (see *Water Purification Methods*).
