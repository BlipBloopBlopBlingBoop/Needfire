# Nutrition Basics for Hard Times

In a shortage the goal shifts from a "balanced diet" to **enough energy plus
enough variety to avoid deficiency disease**. You can run for weeks on limited
food, but missing single nutrients causes specific, preventable illnesses.

## Energy and the macronutrients
- **Calories come first.** An adult needs roughly **2,000 kcal/day** to hold
  weight; hard physical work pushes that far higher. See *Rationing Food and
  Water* for planning and floors.
- **Carbohydrates** (grains, tubers, beans) are the cheapest, most storable
  energy — the backbone of most survival diets.
- **Fats** are the densest energy (9 kcal/g vs 4 for carbs/protein) and carry
  fat-soluble vitamins — prioritise oils, nuts, and fatty meat when you can.
- **Protein** (beans, eggs, fish, meat, dairy) rebuilds the body. Beware
  **"rabbit starvation"**: living on very lean meat alone, with no fat or carbs,
  causes illness — you must have fat or carbohydrate too.

## The deficiency diseases to prevent
- **Vitamin C — scurvy.** Weeks without it: bleeding gums, slow-healing wounds,
  aching joints, exhaustion. Prevent with any fresh plant food — sprouted seeds,
  rose hips, pine-needle tea, raw greens, peppers, citrus, potatoes. **Heat
  destroys vitamin C**, so keep some food raw or lightly cooked.
- **Vitamin A** (night blindness): orange/yellow vegetables, liver, eggs, dark
  greens.
- **B vitamins** (beriberi, pellagra): whole grains, legumes, meat, eggs. Milling
  grain white and boiling maize without lime strips B vitamins — eat whole grains.
- **Vitamin D** (bone weakness): sunlight on skin, oily fish, eggs.
- **Iron** (anaemia): red meat, liver, beans, dark greens; vitamin C aids uptake.
- **Iodine** (goitre): seafood, iodised salt.

## Practical rules
- **Eat variety, even in small amounts.** A handful of many foods beats a lot of
  one. Include something fresh or sprouted for vitamin C.
- **Sprouting** dry beans and seeds creates fresh vitamins from stored staples
  with only water — a key trick when nothing grows.
- **Children, and pregnant or nursing people, need more** protein, energy, and
  micronutrients — protect their rations first.
- Salt and some fat make monotonous rations palatable and are physiological
  needs, not luxuries.

> General reference only, not medical or dietary advice. People with medical
> conditions, and children and pregnant or nursing people, have special needs.
