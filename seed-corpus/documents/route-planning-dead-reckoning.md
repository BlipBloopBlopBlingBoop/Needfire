# Route Planning and Dead Reckoning

Good navigators rarely "find out" where they are — they **never lose track**.
That comes from planning a route in legs, then keeping a running log of every
bearing and distance walked: **dead reckoning**. The toolkit's **Dead-reckoning
log** does the bookkeeping; this page is the craft.

## Plan the route in legs
Break the journey into short legs, each ending at a feature you cannot mistake:
- **Attack points:** navigate coarsely to a big, findable feature near your goal
  (a lake corner, a track junction), then precisely from it to the goal.
- **Handrails:** follow linear features that run your way — a stream, ridge,
  fence, or track beats a compass bearing through blank forest.
- **Catching features:** know what lies *beyond* each target ("if I hit the
  river, I've overshot") so mistakes announce themselves.
- **Aiming off:** when heading for a point on a line (a bridge on a river), aim
  deliberately to one side — on reaching the river you *know* which way to turn.
- For each leg, note on paper: **bearing, distance, estimated time** (see pace
  count and Naismith's rule in *Navigation with Map and Compass*), and an escape
  route if weather or injury forces a retreat.

## Dead reckoning — the running log
From a known starting point, record every leg: **bearing walked and distance
covered** (pace count, or time × speed). Plot each on the map — or enter it in the
toolkit log, which totals your offset from the start and the direct bearing home.
- Log **every** deviation: a detour around a bog is a leg too.
- Estimate a growing **circle of uncertainty** (walking carefully, ~5–10% of
  distance travelled). When the circle gets big, fix your position against a
  feature and restart the log from it.
- Untracked drift is how people get lost: log as you go, not from memory at night.

## Walking an accurate bearing
Sight along the compass to a distinct object **on** the bearing (tree, boulder),
walk to it without watching the needle, repeat. In featureless terrain or fog,
**leapfrog**: send a partner ahead to the edge of visibility, wave them exactly
onto the line, walk to them, repeat. Check the back-bearing to your last point —
it should differ by exactly 180°.

## Boxing an obstacle
To pass a lake or cliff without losing the line: turn **90°**, count paces along
the detour; turn back to the original bearing and pass the obstacle; turn 90° the
opposite way and walk the **same pace count** back; resume. Your dead-reckoning
line continues as if the obstacle weren't there — log the legs anyway.

## Night and fog
Shorten legs, slow down, and trust the compass over your feelings — **in fog and
darkness your sense of direction is wrong within minutes**. Count paces
religiously, use handrails and catching features more, and if conditions collapse,
consider stopping: a controlled wait beats confident travel in a circle.

## If you do get lost
**STOP** — Stop, Think, Observe, Plan (see *Survival Priorities*). Do not wander:
- Backtrack your dead-reckoning log to your last certain position, or
- Move to a nearby unmistakable feature and re-fix, or
- If neither is safe, stay put, make yourself visible (see *Signaling for
  Rescue*), and shelter.
Mark your position before any short scout, and return to it.

> General reference only. Practice pace counting, bearings, and the log on easy
> ground before you must rely on them.
