# Making Soap (Saponification)

Soap is one of the most important products for hygiene and disease prevention. It
is made by reacting fat with a strong alkali — a reaction called saponification.

## The chemistry
Fat (a triglyceride) + lye (sodium hydroxide, NaOH, or potassium hydroxide, KOH)
→ soap (fatty acid salts) + glycerol. NaOH yields hard bar soap; KOH yields soft
or liquid soap. Potassium lye can be leached from hardwood ashes (producing
potash); sodium lye is stronger and more controllable.

## Basic cold-process method
1. **Make or obtain lye.** Commercial NaOH is consistent. Wood-ash lye is variable
   in strength and harder to control.
2. **Measure carefully.** Soap requires the correct fat-to-lye ratio
   (saponification value differs per fat). Too much lye leaves harsh, caustic
   soap; too little leaves greasy, spoiling soap. Use a reliable lye calculator
   or tested recipe for your specific fat.
3. Warm the fat to roughly 40–50 °C. Separately dissolve lye in water — **always
   add lye to water, never water to lye** (it can erupt).
4. Combine at similar temperatures and stir until "trace" (it thickens). Pour
   into moulds, insulate, and let it set 24–48 hours.
5. **Cure** the bars 4–6 weeks in open air; excess water evaporates and any
   remaining reaction completes, making the soap milder and longer-lasting.

## Safety
Lye is **severely caustic** — it burns skin, eyes, and lungs. Wear eye protection
and gloves and work with ventilation. **If lye touches skin or eyes, flush with
large amounts of plain water for at least 15 minutes** — do NOT apply vinegar to
skin (neutralising a caustic on the body releases heat and worsens the burn).
Vinegar is only for neutralising spills on tools and work surfaces. Keep lye and
curing soap away from children and animals.
