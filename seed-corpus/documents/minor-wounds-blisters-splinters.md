# Minor Wounds — Blisters, Splinters, and Small Cuts

Small injuries are the ones you actually get every day, and neglected they turn
into infections that stop you moving. Clean them well, protect them, and watch
for infection (see *Wound Care and Infection*).

## Small cuts and scrapes
1. **Wash your hands**, then **clean the wound** with clean (drinkable/boiled)
   water — irrigate briskly to flush out dirt and grit. Antiseptic around the
   edges is fine; avoid pouring strong antiseptics deep into the wound.
2. **Stop bleeding** with a few minutes of direct pressure.
3. **Cover** with a clean dressing or plaster. Keep it clean and dry; change the
   dressing daily and whenever wet or dirty.
4. **Leave grazes to scab** if clean; keep embedded grit out first, or "tattooing"
   dirt stays under the skin.

## Blisters
Caused by friction and heat — catch them early.
- **Hot spot** (a warm, red, sore patch before a blister forms): stop and
  **cover it now** with tape, moleskin, or a plaster to remove the friction.
  Fix the cause — a wrinkle in a sock, grit in a boot, a loose lace.
- **Intact blister:** usually **leave it unburst** — the skin is a sterile cover.
  Pad around it (a "doughnut" of moleskin) to take the pressure off.
- **If it must be drained** (large, on a pressure point, sure to burst): clean
  the skin, pierce the edge with a flame-sterilised needle, press the fluid out,
  leave the roof of skin on, and dress it.
- **Burst blister:** clean gently, leave the loose skin as a cover if possible,
  and dress it. Watch for infection.

## Splinters and small foreign bodies
- **Clean the area.** Grip a protruding splinter with sterilised tweezers and pull
  **straight out along the angle it went in**.
- A splinter just under the skin can be teased out by nicking the skin over its
  end with a flame-sterilised needle.
- **Leave deep, large, or awkward foreign bodies** (under a nail, in a joint, in
  the eye — see *Eye Injuries*) for someone equipped; forcing them causes damage.
- Flush **grit and debris** out of grazes with plenty of clean water.

## Watch for infection
Increasing **redness, swelling, warmth, throbbing pain, pus, red streaks, or
fever** over the following days means infection — see *Wound Care and Infection*.
Ensure **tetanus protection** is up to date for any wound, especially dirty or
puncture wounds.
