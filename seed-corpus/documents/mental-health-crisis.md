# Mental Health in Crisis

Minds break down under disaster stress just as bodies do, and they respond to
first aid. The aims of psychological first aid are **safety, calm,
connectedness, self-efficacy, and hope**.

## Acute stress is normal
Shaking, numbness, crying, irritability, and insomnia after a frightening event
are normal reactions, not weakness or illness. Most people recover with rest,
safety, and human contact. Say so, plainly.

## Grounding techniques
- **5-4-3-2-1**: name 5 things you see, 4 you can touch, 3 you hear, 2 you
  smell, 1 you taste. This anchors a panicking mind in the present.
- **Slow exhale breathing**: breathe in for a count of four, out slowly for six
  or more. The long exhale calms the body directly.

## Helping someone in crisis
Listen without trying to fix. Do not argue with panic or grief — sit with the
person, speak slowly, use their name. Give simple, concrete tasks ("hold this",
"fetch water"); useful action restores a sense of control.

## Suicide risk
**Asking directly about suicide does not increase the risk.** Ask plainly:
"Are you thinking about killing yourself?" If yes: remove means (weapons,
medications, ropes), do not leave the person alone, and arrange ongoing watch
and support. Take every statement of intent seriously.

## Group morale
Keep daily routines and shared meals. Give everyone a role. Share honest
information — rumor and silence corrode trust faster than bad news.

## Children
Keep their routines as normal as possible. Answer questions simply and
truthfully, without graphic detail. Let them help with real tasks; being useful
protects them too.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
