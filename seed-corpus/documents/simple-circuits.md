# Basic Electronics and Ohm's Law

A little electronics knowledge lets you repair, build, and safely wire low-voltage
systems like lighting, radios, and chargers.

## Ohm's Law — the foundation
Voltage (V, volts) = Current (I, amps) × Resistance (R, ohms): **V = I × R**.
Rearranged: I = V / R, and R = V / I.

Power (P, watts) = Voltage × Current: **P = V × I**. This tells you heat and
consumption — a 12 V device drawing 2 A uses 24 W.

## Series vs parallel
- **Series**: components end-to-end. The same current flows through all; voltages
  add. Resistances add: R = R1 + R2 + …
- **Parallel**: components across the same two points. Voltage is the same across
  each; currents add. For resistors, 1/R = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + …

Batteries in series add voltage (two 6 V → 12 V); in parallel add capacity at the
same voltage.

## Practical wiring
- Match wire gauge to current: thin wire overheats under high current. Thicker
  (lower-gauge) wire carries more amps safely.
- **Fuse** circuits near the source so a short blows the fuse instead of starting
  a fire. Size the fuse just above normal current draw.
- Observe polarity (+ / −) for DC devices; reversing it can destroy electronics.
- A multimeter measures voltage, current, resistance, and continuity — the single
  most useful diagnostic tool.

## Safety
Low-voltage DC (12 V) is generally safe to touch but can still cause fires and
burns through shorts. Treat mains AC and any high-voltage source as lethal; do not
work on it without proper training and isolation.
