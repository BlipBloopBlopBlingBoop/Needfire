# Rainwater Harvesting and Water Storage

Rain is the easiest clean water to collect in most climates, and stored water is
what carries you through dry spells and disrupted supply. Collect it cleanly,
store it safely, and treat it before drinking.

## Collecting rain
- **Catchment.** Any clean, non-toxic surface works — a tarp strung as a funnel,
  a tent fly, or a roof. Avoid surfaces with lead paint, tar/asphalt shingles,
  treated wood, or bird-heavy areas; these leach contaminants.
- **First flush.** The first rain rinses dust, droppings, and debris off the
  catchment. **Divert the first few litres away** before you start collecting the
  clean flow — a simple diverter pipe or just moving the container over once the
  surface has rinsed.
- **Screen the inlet** with cloth or fine mesh to keep out leaves, insects, and
  mosquito larvae.

## Storing water safely
- Use **food-grade containers** (marked HDPE #2, or jugs that held drinking water
  or juice). **Never reuse containers that held chemicals, fuel, or milk** —
  milk residue spoils and cannot be fully cleaned out.
- **Keep it dark and cool.** Sunlight breeds algae; heat encourages growth. Store
  out of direct sun, and keep containers sealed against insects and dust.
- **Label and date** every container. Rotate drinking-water stores about every
  6–12 months; long-stored water is fine to drink after re-treating, but tastes
  flat (pouring between containers re-aerates it).
- Raise containers off bare concrete on wood, and never store water where a leak
  or contamination can reach it from below.

## Making it safe to drink
Harvested and stored water is **not automatically safe** — treat it as suspect.
Filter out sediment, then disinfect (boil, or dose with plain bleach, or filter
plus a chemical step) exactly as for any field water. See *Water Purification
Methods*. For long storage, a small maintenance dose of unscented bleach
(about 2 drops of 5–6% bleach per litre) keeps water disinfected.

## Rough yields
A catchment collects about **1 litre per square metre for every 1 mm of rain**
(roughly 0.6 gallons per square foot per inch). Even a 3 × 4 m tarp in a 10 mm
shower yields ~120 litres — size your storage to your roof and your rainfall.

> General reference only, not professional advice. Always treat harvested water
> before drinking; when in doubt, use more than one method.
