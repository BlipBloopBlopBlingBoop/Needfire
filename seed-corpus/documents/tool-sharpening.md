# Sharpening and Maintaining Tools

A sharp, maintained tool is safer and far more effective than a dull one — dull
blades require force and slip unpredictably, causing most knife injuries.

## Sharpening a knife or blade
1. Use a sharpening stone (whetstone). Coarse grit repairs and reshapes; fine grit
   refines and polishes the edge. Wet the stone with water or oil as appropriate.
2. Hold a consistent angle — roughly 15–20° per side for most knives. Consistency
   matters more than the exact number.
3. Push the edge across the stone as if shaving a thin layer off it, alternating
   sides evenly. Work from coarse to fine grit.
4. Finish by stropping on leather to remove the burr and polish the edge.
5. Test gently: a sharp edge bites into a fingernail or slices paper cleanly.

## Axes, hatchets, and tools
File the edge maintaining the original bevel; remove nicks first with coarser
strokes, then refine. Keep the handle tight; swelling a loose head with a soaked
wedge is a temporary fix.

## Rust and storage
- Remove rust with abrasive (sandpaper, steel wool) and oil.
- Wipe steel tools with a light oil film before storage to prevent rust.
- Store dry; keep edges protected (sheath or guard) so they stay sharp and safe.

## Safety
Always cut away from your body and hands. Keep your supporting hand behind the
edge's path. A controlled, sharp tool with good technique is the safest.
