# Safe Medication Use

When medicines are scavenged or stockpiled rather than prescribed, discipline
prevents poisoning. Check the **five rights** every single time: right person,
right drug, right dose, right route, right time.

## Expired medications
Most tablets and capsules retain much of their potency for years past the
printed date if stored cool, dry, and dark. Degrading forms are different:
- **Liquids, suspensions, eye drops, insulin, and epinephrine lose potency much
  faster.** Never use a liquid that is cloudy, discolored, or separated.
- **Do not use tetracycline-class antibiotics that are expired or degraded** —
  historical caution: degraded tetracycline has caused kidney harm.

## Before giving any drug
- **Ask about allergies every time**, especially to penicillins and sulfa drugs.
- Confirm the drug's identity from the marking or packaging; never guess from a
  loose pill's appearance alone.

## Children
**Pediatric dosing is weight-based.** Never guess by giving a fraction of an
adult dose without a written reference — for some drugs that guess is toxic.
Weigh the child, look up mg per kg, and calculate.

## Antibiotics
Use only for likely bacterial infection, choose the right spectrum from a
reference (the WHO essential medicines model list is a sound base), and
complete the course. Half-courses breed resistant infections that endanger the
whole group.

## Pain
Follow a ladder: start with paracetamol or ibuprofen, escalate only if
inadequate, and use the lowest dose that works.

## Write it down
Document what was given, to whom, how much, when, and any reaction. Memory
fails under stress; records prevent double-dosing and reveal allergies.
