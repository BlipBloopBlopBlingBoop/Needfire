# Stroke — Recognize It FAST

A stroke is a "brain attack": blood flow to part of the brain is suddenly cut
off (a clot) or a vessel bursts (a bleed). Brain cells die by the minute, so the
whole game is **recognise it fast and get advanced care fast** — the treatments
that save brain only work in a short window.

## The FAST test
Any ONE of these appearing suddenly means treat as a stroke:
- **F — Face.** Ask them to smile. Does one side of the face droop or look
  uneven?
- **A — Arms.** Ask them to raise both arms. Does one drift down or can't lift?
- **S — Speech.** Ask them to repeat a simple sentence. Is it slurred, garbled,
  or are they unable to find words?
- **T — Time.** If any of these are present, **note the time symptoms started**
  and get emergency help immediately. Time lost is brain lost.

Other sudden signs: numbness or weakness on one side, confusion, trouble seeing
in one or both eyes, severe unexplained headache ("worst of my life"), dizziness,
or loss of balance.

## What to do
1. **Get advanced medical help now.** Tell them it may be a stroke and give the
   time symptoms began — it decides which treatments are still possible.
2. **Note the start time** precisely (or the last time the person was seen
   normal, e.g. before sleep).
3. Keep the person **still, calm, and comfortable.** If fully alert, they may sit
   propped up.
4. **Nothing to eat or drink** — a stroke can paralyse swallowing and cause
   choking, and they may need urgent procedures.
5. If they become **unresponsive but are breathing**, place them in the recovery
   position (on their side). If breathing stops, start CPR.

## Do not
- Do not give aspirin — unlike a heart attack, a stroke may be a bleed, and
  aspirin can make a bleed worse.
- Do not wait to "see if it passes." Symptoms that fade (a TIA / mini-stroke)
  are a serious warning and still need urgent assessment.

> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
