# Snakebite Treatment

Most snakebites are survivable — even without antivenom — if the person stays
calm and gets good supportive care, and if you avoid the folk "treatments" that
do real harm. Move away from the snake, treat every bite as venomous, and settle
in to manage it over hours. **Panic and exertion pump venom through the body
faster — your first medicine is calm.**

## Do these first
1. **Keep the person calm and completely still.** Sit or lie them down; carry
   them rather than let them walk. Movement spreads venom.
2. **Immobilise the bitten limb** with a splint and keep it at about **heart
   level** (not raised high, not hanging down).
3. **Remove rings, watches, and tight clothing now**, before swelling makes it
   impossible — swelling can cut off circulation.
4. **Mark the leading edge of swelling/redness** with a pen and write the time;
   re-mark every 15–30 minutes to track how fast it spreads.
5. **Gently clean and cover** the bite. Note the time of the bite.

## Do NOT
**Do not cut the wound, suck out venom (by mouth or a "venom extractor"), apply a
tourniquet, apply ice, give alcohol, or give aspirin/ibuprofen.** These worsen
tissue damage, bleeding, or shock, and none remove venom. Do not try to catch or
kill the snake.

## The two kinds of venom, and what you'll see
- **Tissue/blood venom (vipers, pit vipers, rattlesnakes, adders):** severe local
  pain, swelling, blistering, bruising; later, bleeding (gums, wounds, blood in
  urine/vomit) and shock. **Do NOT apply a pressure bandage** — trapping this
  venom concentrates tissue destruction.
- **Nerve venom (cobras, kraits, coral snakes, mambas, Australian elapids):** often
  little local swelling, but **drooping eyelids, double vision, slurred speech,
  drooling, then weakness of the muscles you breathe with**. For these, a
  **pressure-immobilisation bandage** (below) buys time.

## Pressure-immobilisation bandage — neurotoxic snakes only
If the snake is a known **neurotoxic** type, firmly wrap the **whole limb** (snug
as for a sprain, from the toes/fingers upward) over the bite and splint it. This
slows paralysing venom through the lymph. **Never use it for vipers/rattlesnakes.**
If you truly cannot identify the snake, base it on the signs: use it if paralysis
signs appear; do not if there is marked local swelling and bruising.

## Off-grid: managing without antivenom
Antivenom is the only cure, but many people survive on supportive care alone.
While you evacuate — or if you cannot:
- **Keep them still, warm, and hydrated** with small sips if fully alert.
- **Watch breathing and the airway constantly** with neurotoxic bites. If the
  breathing muscles fail, **breathe for them** (rescue breaths / CPR) — people
  have been kept alive for many hours this way until the venom wore off.
- **Pain:** paracetamol/acetaminophen only — **never aspirin or ibuprofen** (they
  worsen bleeding).
- **Watch the limb:** extreme tightness, a pulseless cold hand/foot, or spreading
  blackened tissue is limb-threatening — loosen anything constricting and evacuate.
- **Watch for bleeding and shock** with viper bites; treat shock (lie flat, legs
  up, keep warm — see *Shock — Recognition and First Aid*).
- **Keep the wound clean;** infection sets in over days. Ensure tetanus cover.
- **Do not give food or drink to a drowsy person.**

## Get to antivenom
Evacuate to medical care even if symptoms seem mild — envenomation can worsen over
hours. Note the snake's colour, pattern, and size **from a safe distance** (a
photo is ideal). Some bites inject no venom ("dry bites") — swelling and symptoms
never develop — but **never assume you got one**; watch for at least 24 hours.
