# Reading Topographic Maps

A topographic map is the terrain drawn flat: its contour lines encode every hill,
valley, and cliff. Reading one fluently — and pinning a position with a grid
reference — is the core navigation skill that works with no batteries at all.
Keep paper maps of your home region in the kit, in a waterproof bag.

## Scale — how far is that?
The scale says how much the world is shrunk: on a **1:25,000** map, 1 cm = 250 m;
on **1:50,000**, 1 cm = 500 m. Small-number scales show more detail over less
area. Measure a route (not straight-line) by laying a string or the marked edge
of a paper along it, then reading it against the scale bar. Most maps' grid
squares are **1 km** on a side — count squares for a fast estimate.

## Contour lines — the shape of the land
Each brown contour joins points of equal elevation, a fixed **contour interval**
(printed in the legend, e.g. 10 m) apart.
- **Close together = steep; far apart = gentle; touching = cliff.**
- **Closed rings = a summit** (or a depression if tick-marked inward).
- **V-shapes point uphill in valleys** — a V crossing a stream points upstream.
  Ridges show as U/V shapes pointing downhill.
- A **saddle/col** (low point between two summits) looks like an hourglass.
- Follow one heavier **index contour** and read its printed elevation to anchor
  yourself vertically.

## Colours and symbols
Blue = water; green = vegetated; white = open; brown = contours; black =
human-made (buildings, tracks, boundaries); red = major roads. **Always read the
map's own legend** — symbols differ by country and series. Note the map's date:
tracks, buildings, even shorelines change.

## Grid references — naming a point
Maps carry a numbered grid (usually **UTM/kilometre squares**, or latitude/
longitude in the margins). The universal rule: **read RIGHT, then UP** — eastings
before northings ("in the door, up the stairs").
- A **4-figure** reference (e.g. `23 41`) names a 1 km square; **6-figure**
  (`234 415`, estimating tenths) pins ~100 m; **8-figure** ~10 m.
- Quote the **zone and full numbers** when passing a position by radio, and say
  which map/datum you're using. The toolkit's **Grid converter** turns latitude/
  longitude into UTM eastings/northings and back.

## Orient the map, then trust it
- **By compass:** rotate the map until its north lines align with the needle,
  remembering declination — the margin diagram gives the local value (see
  *Navigation with Map and Compass*).
- **By terrain:** rotate the map until what you see matches what it draws — the
  lake to your left is on your left. An oriented map turns reading into looking.
- **Terrain association:** keep a running match between map and ground —
  "crossing the stream now, spur on my right" — so you are never more than one
  feature from a known position.

## Care in the field
Fold the map to your area of travel, case it in a clear waterproof bag you can
read through, and mark your route and escape lines in pencil. A soaked, torn map
navigates no one — and carry the *adjacent* sheets: emergencies rarely respect
map edges.

> General reference only. Map symbols, grids, and datums vary by country and
> publisher — the legend on your own map is the authority.
