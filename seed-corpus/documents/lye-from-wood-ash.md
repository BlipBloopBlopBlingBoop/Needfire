# Lye and Potash from Wood Ash

Lye is the strong alkali that makes soap, treats corn (nixtamalisation), and
cleans. You can leach a usable lye (mostly **potassium carbonate/hydroxide**,
"potash") from ordinary hardwood ash — a foundational rebuild-stack skill that
turns a waste product into a chemical feedstock. **Lye is caustic and burns skin,
eyes, and lungs — treat it with real respect.**

## What you get
- **Wood-ash lye** is mainly potassium-based (potash). It makes a soft or liquid
  soap and works for many cleaning and food uses.
- Industrial/commercial lye is usually **sodium hydroxide** (from the chlor-alkali
  process), which makes hard bar soap. Wood ash gives you the potassium version.
- **Hardwood ash** (oak, ash, fruit woods) yields far more alkali than softwood or
  conifer ash. Use clean ash from untreated wood only.

## Leaching lye (traditional method)
1. **Build an ash hopper:** a barrel or wooden V-trough with a drainage hole at
   the bottom, lined with straw or cloth to filter.
2. Pack it with **hardwood ashes**; add a layer of gravel/straw at the base.
3. **Slowly pour soft water** (rainwater is ideal) through the ashes and collect
   the brown liquid draining out — that is the lye. Re-pour it through fresh ash
   to strengthen it.
4. **Test the strength:** traditional test — the lye is strong enough for soap
   when a fresh egg or potato **floats with a coin-sized patch above the surface**.
   Too weak: leach again or boil down to concentrate. Too strong: dilute.

## Uses
- **Soap:** combine lye with fat/oil to saponify — see *Making Soap
  (Saponification)* for ratios and method (get the lye:fat balance right or the
  soap is caustic or greasy).
- **Nixtamalisation:** simmering maize in a weak lye/ash solution loosens the hulls
  and, crucially, **frees the vitamin B3 (niacin)** — preventing pellagra in
  maize-dependent diets.
- **Cleaning:** dilute lye cuts grease.

## Safety — this chemical is dangerous
- **Wear eye protection and gloves;** cover skin. Lye causes deep burns and
  blinds.
- **Always add lye to water, never water to lye**, and expect **heat** as it
  dissolves. Work outdoors or with strong ventilation — the fumes harm lungs.
- Use **heat-resistant, non-reactive containers** (stainless steel, heavy plastic,
  glass, or wood — **never aluminium**, which lye attacks and releases hydrogen).
- **Neutralise splashes** by flushing with lots of water (and vinegar on surfaces,
  not deep in eyes); rinse eyes/skin with water for many minutes and seek help.
- Label and store securely, far from food and children.
