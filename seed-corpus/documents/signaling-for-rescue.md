# Signaling for Rescue

Searchers can only find what they can detect. Make yourself loud, bright, big —
and stationary.

## The rule of threes
**Three of anything means distress**: three fires or smoke columns, three
whistle blasts, three flashes. Repeat in groups of three, pause, repeat.

## Signal mirror
A mirror flash is visible for many kilometres and can catch an aircraft crew's
eye. Aim it: extend your free hand and make a V with two fingers, put the
target (aircraft, vehicle, distant ridge) in the V, then tilt the mirror so the
flash plays across your fingers — it is now on target. Sweep the horizon
periodically even with no target in sight. Any shiny surface works: polished
metal, foil, a phone screen.

## Ground-to-air symbols
Build symbols searchers know: **V = need assistance, X = need medical help, an
arrow = traveling this way.** Make them big and high-contrast — strokes at
least 3 m long, from rocks, logs, trenches, or bright material against a plain
background.

## Fire and smoke
Smoke by day: get a hot fire going, then add green vegetation for white smoke
or rubber/oil for black — choose whichever contrasts with the sky and terrain.
Flame by night. Keep signal fires prepared and ready to light fast.

## Sound
A whistle carries much farther than shouting and never wears out your voice.
Three blasts, pause, repeat.

## Stay put and get bigger
**Once someone knows to look for you, stay where you are** — searchers find
stationary targets far more easily than moving ones. Spread bright items,
move in the open when aircraft pass, and use long shadows at dawn and dusk.

## Phones
Even with "no service", a phone may still connect to 911/112 through any
network. Try from high ground, then power off between attempts to save
battery.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained professional care. If you
> can reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
