# Controlling Severe Bleeding

Severe bleeding can kill in minutes, so it comes before almost everything except
an unsafe scene and a blocked airway. The whole game is **pressure onto the
bleeding point** — direct pressure, wound packing, then a tourniquet if a limb
won't stop. Off-grid, you must also keep the wound managed for days and know when
a tourniquet can be safely swapped for a dressing.

## Act immediately
Protect yourself from blood contact if you can grab gloves or a barrier in
seconds, but **do not delay a spurting bleed** to find one. Expose the wound so
you can see where the blood is actually coming from.

## 1. Direct pressure — the first and best tool
Press **hard** directly on the wound with a clean cloth or your bare hand, using
your body weight. **Do not lift off to look** — peeking breaks the forming clot.
Hold firm, constant pressure for a full **10 minutes** before easing off. Most
bleeding stops here.

## 2. Pack deep or gaping wounds
For a deep wound you can't close with surface pressure, **pack it**: push clean
gauze or cloth firmly **into** the wound, right down onto the bleeding vessel,
then press hard on top. Keep stuffing more material in if it soaks — never pull
the packing back out. Then bind a **firm pressure bandage** over it to hold the
pressure. If a dressing soaks through, **add more on top; do not remove it.**

## 3. Tourniquet — for a limb that won't stop
If a life-threatening bleed from an **arm or leg** won't stop with pressure and
packing, apply a tourniquet:
- **High and tight:** 5–7 cm (2–3 in) above the wound, **not over a joint**.
- **Tighten until the bleeding STOPS** and you can't feel a pulse below it. A
  tourniquet that only slows the bleeding makes things worse (it blocks veins but
  not arteries) — tighten more or add a second one just above the first.
- **Note the time.** Write it on the skin. **Do not loosen it to peek.**
- **Improvise** with a band **at least 5 cm wide** (cloth strip, belt, triangular
  bandage — never thin cord or wire) and a strong stick as a **windlass**: tie the
  band, tie the stick across it, twist to tighten, then secure the stick.
- **Amputation or a mangled limb:** tourniquet and leave it.

### Off-grid: converting a tourniquet
A tourniquet is meant for ~2 hours; beyond that the limb is at risk. If help is
many hours or days away, the tourniquet has been on **under ~2 hours**, the
bleeding is fully controlled, and the limb is not mangled or amputated, you can
try to **convert it to a packed pressure dressing**: pack and firmly bandage the
wound, then **slowly loosen** the tourniquet (leave it in place, loose) and watch
closely. If bleeding restarts, **re-tighten immediately and leave it on.** Do NOT
attempt conversion if you can't watch constantly, if it's been on more than ~6
hours, or if the person is in shock — leave it on and evacuate.

## Wounds where a tourniquet can't go
- **Neck, armpit, groin (junctional):** you can't tie a tourniquet here. **Pack
  the wound hard** and press with body weight; keep packing more in. Never tie
  anything around the neck — press directly on the wound only.
- **Chest that sucks/bubbles air:** cover with something airtight taped on **three
  sides** (a vent flap).
- **Abdomen with organs out:** do NOT push them back; cover with a clean,
  water-moistened cloth.
- **Chest/abdomen/pelvis internal bleeding:** cannot be pressure-controlled — keep
  still and flat, treat for shock, evacuate fast.

## Treat for shock
Blood loss causes shock: pale, cold, clammy, grey skin; fast weak pulse; fast
breathing; thirst; restlessness then drowsiness. Lay them flat, **raise the legs**
(if no leg/spine injury), keep them **warm** and off cold ground, and reassure
them. If fully alert with no belly wound and care is far off, small sips of water
or oral rehydration solution help — stop if they feel sick. Nothing by mouth if
drowsy or a belly injury is possible. See *Shock — Recognition and First Aid*.

## After bleeding is controlled
Immobilise the part and keep it slightly raised. Leave a clot-holding packing
undisturbed; change only the soaked outer layers. Over the next days watch for
**infection** — spreading redness, swelling, heat, pus, fever — and manage it (see
*Wound Care and Infection*). Ensure tetanus protection is current. **Anyone who
needed a tourniquet or wound packing must be evacuated to real medical care.**
