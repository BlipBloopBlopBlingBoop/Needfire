# Wound Care and Infection

Once bleeding is controlled, the enemy is infection. In a setting without
antibiotics, how you clean a wound in the first hour matters more than
anything you do later.

## Irrigation — the single most important step
**Flush the wound generously with drinking-quality water under gentle
pressure.** Fill a clean plastic bag and puncture a pinhole, or use a syringe,
and direct the stream into the wound — a litre or more for a dirty wound.
**Remove all visible debris** with clean tweezers. Dirt left inside will
become infection. **Wash your hands before and after** any wound care.

## Dressing — do not seal in trouble
**Do NOT close a dirty wound tight.** A wound stitched or taped shut over
contamination becomes an abscess. Instead:
- Cover with a **clean moist-to-dry dressing** (damp gauze against the wound,
  dry layers on top).
- **Reassess daily** and change dressings whenever they are wet or dirty.
- Only a fresh, thoroughly cleaned, low-risk cut should be pulled closed, and
  even then watch it closely.

## Signs of infection
- **Increasing pain after day 2** (normal wounds hurt less each day),
- Spreading redness, warmth, swelling, pus.

**Red streaks running from the wound toward the trunk, or fever, mean the
infection is spreading through the body.** Without antibiotics this is
life-threatening — reaching medical care becomes the priority. Meanwhile:
open and drain any abscess, flush again, elevate, and dress.

## Other notes
- **Deep, dirty, or puncture wounds carry tetanus risk** — another reason to
  reach care if vaccination is not current.
- **Honey** spread thinly on a superficial wound is a historical adjunct with
  some antibacterial effect — a supplement to cleaning, never a cure.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
