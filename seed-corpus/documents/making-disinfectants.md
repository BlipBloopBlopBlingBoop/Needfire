# Making and Using Disinfectants

Clean surfaces, tools, and hands break the chain of disease that fells more people
in disasters than injuries do. A little chemistry stretches scarce supplies into a
lot of disinfection. (For making water safe to **drink**, use the smaller doses in
*Water Purification Methods* — this page is about surfaces and hygiene.)

## Chlorine bleach — the workhorse
Plain, unscented household **bleach (sodium hypochlorite, 5–6%)** disinfects
surfaces, dishes, and sickroom spills. **Mix fresh** — diluted bleach loses
strength within about a day, and full-strength bleach also degrades over months,
especially warm.
- **General surfaces / dishes:** about **1 part bleach to 100 parts water**
  (roughly **2 teaspoons of 5–6% bleach per litre**, ~2.5 tablespoons per US gallon).
  Wet the surface, leave it **at least 1–2 minutes** (longer is better), then air
  dry.
- **Blood, vomit, faeces, or sickroom spills:** a strong **1 part bleach to 10
  parts water** (~100 mL per litre). Wear gloves; wipe up the bulk first, apply,
  leave several minutes, then clean.
- **Rules:** clean visible dirt off first (organic matter uses up chlorine);
  **never mix bleach with ammonia or acids** (toxic gases); ventilate; label the
  container; keep away from children. Bleach corrodes metal and bleaches fabric.

## Alcohol
**60–80% alcohol** (ethanol or isopropyl) kills most germs on skin and small hard
surfaces and dries clean — good for hands and equipment when strong enough. Below
~60% it is much weaker. Alcohol is flammable; keep it from flame.

## Soap and heat — do not overlook them
- **Soap and water** physically remove germs and are the first choice for
  handwashing and general cleaning — see *Making Soap (Saponification)*.
- **Boiling** disinfects water, cloth, and small implements when chemicals are
  short.
- **Sunlight/UV** and thorough drying reduce contamination on surfaces and cloth.

## Other disinfectants
Iodine solutions disinfect skin and (in the right dose) water. Hydrogen peroxide
(3%) cleans surfaces. Lime (calcium hydroxide) and wood ash raise pH and are used
to treat latrines and organic waste (see *Sanitation and Latrines*).

## Safety
Store disinfectants labelled, sealed, out of children's reach, and **never in
food or drink containers**. Use with ventilation and gloves; keep incompatible
chemicals apart.
