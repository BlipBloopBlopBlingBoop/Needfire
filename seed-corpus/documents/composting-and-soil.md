# Composting and Building Soil

Healthy soil is the foundation of growing food, and compost is how you make and
keep it fertile without bought fertiliser — recycling waste into next season's
harvest. Feed the soil, and the soil feeds the plants.

## What good soil is
Living soil is crumbly, dark, holds moisture yet drains, and is full of roots,
worms, and microbes. Plants need it loose (for roots and air), fertile (nutrients),
and alive. You build all three with **organic matter** — compost, manure, and
mulch.

## Composting — the greens-and-browns recipe
Compost is controlled rotting. Balance two ingredients:
- **"Greens" (nitrogen):** fresh material — kitchen scraps, green leaves, grass
  clippings, manure.
- **"Browns" (carbon):** dry material — straw, dry leaves, cardboard, wood chips,
  paper.
Aim for **more browns than greens by volume** (very roughly 2–3 parts brown to 1
part green). Too much green goes slimy and stinks; too much brown just sits.

**Method:**
1. Build a pile at least ~1 m cube (smaller struggles to heat).
2. **Layer or mix** greens and browns; keep it **as damp as a wrung-out sponge**.
3. **Turn it** every week or two to add air — air keeps it hot and odour-free.
4. A hot pile (steaming, too hot to hold your hand in) kills weed seeds and
   pathogens and finishes in weeks; a cold pile just takes months.
It is ready when it is dark, crumbly, earthy-smelling, and the ingredients are
unrecognisable.

## Do and don't
- **Compost:** vegetable scraps, coffee grounds, eggshells, plant trimmings,
  manure from plant-eating animals, straw, leaves, paper.
- **Avoid / be careful:** meat, fat, and dairy (attract pests, smell) unless hot-
  composted; diseased plants; pet/human waste unless properly, hotly, and long
  composted. **Human waste ("humanure") needs long, hot, careful composting** to be
  safe — otherwise treat it as latrine waste (see *Sanitation and Latrines*).

## Feeding soil without a compost pile
- **Mulch** the surface with leaves, straw, or grass — it feeds soil life, holds
  water, and suppresses weeds as it breaks down.
- **Manure** from herbivores (aged/composted, not fresh — fresh can burn plants and
  carry pathogens).
- **Wood ash** adds potassium and lime (raises pH) — use sparingly.
- **Cover crops / green manure** (clover, beans) grown and turned in add nitrogen
  and organic matter; legumes fix nitrogen from the air.
- **Rotate crops** and avoid leaving soil bare — bare soil erodes and dies.

> General reference only. Composting human or animal waste safely requires
> understanding pathogens and adequate time and temperature; when unsure, treat
> waste as hazardous.
