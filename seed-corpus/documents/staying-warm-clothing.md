# Staying Warm — Clothing and Insulation

Most of your warmth in the cold comes from **trapped still air**, not from the
fabric itself. Managing that air — and staying dry — matters more than any single
garment. Heat loss kills faster than hunger or thirst (see *Shelter and
Hypothermia*).

## The layering system
- **Base layer** (against the skin): wicks sweat away. Wool or synthetics.
  **Avoid cotton next to the skin in the cold** — "cotton kills" because it soaks
  up sweat and loses nearly all its insulation when wet.
- **Mid layer** (insulation): the warm, lofty layer that traps air — wool, fleece,
  or down. Add or remove mid layers to regulate temperature.
- **Outer layer** (shell): blocks wind and rain. A garment that stops wind can be
  worth more than a thick one that lets it through.

## The rules of warmth
- **Stay dry — inside and out.** Wet from rain OR from your own sweat both steal
  heat. **Vent and shed layers before you sweat** on exertion, then add them back
  when you stop. Dry wet clothing at the first chance.
- **Trap air.** Loose, layered, lofted clothing insulates; tight or crushed
  clothing (packed down, sat-on) does not. Do not lace boots so tight they cut
  circulation.
- **Cover the extremities.** A lot of heat leaves through an uncovered head,
  neck, and hands. A hat, hood, and neck gaiter are cheap, huge wins. Keep hands
  and feet dry; mittens beat gloves for warmth.
- **Insulate from the ground.** Sitting or lying on cold ground drains heat fast
  — put a pad, branches, or a pack under you.

## Improvising insulation
Stuff dry leaves, dry grass, moss, feathers, or crumpled paper between two layers
of clothing to create dead-air space. Newspaper under a shirt or in boots adds
real warmth. A large plastic bag or space blanket as an outer shell blocks wind
and traps radiant heat — but vent it so sweat does not soak everything.

## Warning
Shivering that **stops**, with confusion or drowsiness, is worsening hypothermia,
not recovery — get the person into shelter, dry layers, and gentle warmth, and
seek help. See *Shelter and Hypothermia* and *Frostbite and Cold Injuries*.

> General reference only, not professional advice. In severe cold, prevention and
> early action matter far more than any single piece of gear.
