# Choking and Blocked Airway

A choking person may clutch their throat, be unable to speak, cough weakly or
not at all, and turn blue. If they can cough or speak, the airway is only
partly blocked — do not interfere yet.

## Mild obstruction (can cough or speak)
**Encourage them to keep coughing.** A strong cough clears an airway better
than anything you can do. Stay with them and watch for worsening.

## Severe obstruction — adult or child (cannot breathe, cough, or speak)
1. **Give 5 back blows**: lean them forward and strike sharply between the
   shoulder blades with the heel of your hand. Check after each blow.
2. **Give 5 abdominal thrusts**: stand behind them, fist just above the navel,
   grasp it with your other hand, and pull sharply inward and upward.
3. **Alternate** 5 back blows and 5 abdominal thrusts until the object comes
   out or the person collapses.
- For a **pregnant or very large person**, use **chest thrusts** instead:
  fist on the centre of the breastbone, pull straight back.

## Infants (under 1 year)
1. Lay the infant face-down along your forearm, head lower than chest, and
   give **5 back blows** between the shoulder blades.
2. Turn face-up and give **5 chest thrusts** with two fingers on the centre of
   the chest, sharper and slower than CPR compressions.
3. Alternate until the object clears. **Never give an infant abdominal
   thrusts.**

## If they become unconscious
Lower them to the ground and **start CPR** with chest compressions. Each time
you open the mouth to give breaths, **look inside** and remove any object you
can actually see. **Never do a blind finger sweep** — it can push the object
deeper.

Anyone who received abdominal thrusts should be checked for internal injury
when care is available.

> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
