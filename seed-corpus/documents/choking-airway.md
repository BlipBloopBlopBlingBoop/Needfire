# Choking and Blocked Airway

A blocked airway kills in minutes, but a few seconds of the right action usually
clears it. The one decision that matters first: **can any air move?** A person who
can cough, speak, or cry has a *partial* block and their own cough is the best
tool. A silent person who cannot breathe has a *complete* block and needs you now.

## Mild block — can cough or speak
**Encourage them to keep coughing.** Do not slap the back or give thrusts to
someone coughing effectively — you could dislodge a partial block into a complete
one. Stay with them and watch; if they weaken and go silent, act as below.

## Complete block — adult or child (silent, can't breathe, going blue)
Alternate, checking the mouth after each set and removing only what you can see:
1. **5 sharp back blows** — lean them well forward; strike between the shoulder
   blades with the heel of your hand.
2. **5 abdominal thrusts (Heimlich)** — stand behind, fist just above the navel,
   grasp with the other hand, pull sharply **in and up**.
3. Repeat 5-and-5 until the object comes out or they collapse.
- **Pregnant or very large person:** use **chest thrusts** (fist on the centre of
  the breastbone, pull straight back) instead of abdominal thrusts.

## Infant (under 1 year)
**Never give an infant abdominal thrusts.** Alternate:
1. Face-down along your forearm, head lower than the body — **5 back blows**
   between the shoulder blades.
2. Turn face-up — **5 chest thrusts** with two fingers on the breastbone (sharper
   and deeper than CPR compressions).
Repeat until it clears or the infant goes limp.

## If you are choking and alone
Give **yourself abdominal thrusts** — a fist above the navel, thrust in and up — or
**press your upper belly hard and fast over a firm edge**: the back of a chair, a
railing, a rock, a fence rail. Repeat until the airway clears. Get to where someone
might find you if you feel you may pass out.

## If they become unconscious
Lower them gently to the ground and **start CPR** (see *CPR for Adults, Children,
and Infants*). The chest compressions help push the blockage out. Each time you
open the mouth for breaths, **look inside and remove the object only if you can see
it**. **Never do a blind finger sweep** — it drives the object deeper.

## After the airway clears
Let them rest; a sore throat or cough is normal for a while. **Anyone who received
abdominal thrusts should be checked for internal injury** when care is available.
Seek help if there is ongoing trouble swallowing or breathing, a persistent cough,
drooling, chest pain, or a feeling that something is still stuck — a piece may have
passed into the lung and can cause infection days later.

> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
