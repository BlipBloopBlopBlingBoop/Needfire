# Seizure Response

Most seizures stop on their own within 1–3 minutes. Your job is to **protect the
person from injury** while it runs its course, keep the airway clear afterward, and
recognise the few situations that turn a seizure into an emergency.

## During the seizure
1. **Protect, don't restrain.** Move hard or sharp objects away, or ease the person
   away from danger (fire, water, edges, machinery). **Do not hold them down** —
   you can't stop the seizure and may cause injury.
2. **Cushion the head** with something soft — a folded jacket, your hands.
3. **Put NOTHING in the mouth.** A person cannot swallow their tongue; forcing
   objects in breaks teeth and blocks the airway.
4. **Loosen anything tight** around the neck.
5. **Time it from the start.** Duration is the key decision.

## When it's an emergency
Get to help / rescue medication any way you can if:
- The active seizure lasts **more than 5 minutes**, or seizures come **back-to-back
  without waking** in between — this is **status epilepticus**, life-threatening,
  and may not self-stop. If you have prescribed rescue medication (e.g. buccal
  midazolam) and know how, this is when to use it.
- It is a **first-ever** seizure.
- It happens **in water**, causes **injury**, or the person is **pregnant**.
- They **do not wake** within ~15–20 minutes of the shaking stopping, or breathing
  looks abnormal after.

## After the shaking stops
Roll them into the **recovery position** (on their side, head tilted slightly back)
so saliva or vomit drains. They will be confused, drowsy, maybe agitated
("postictal") — this is normal and can last up to an hour. **Stay with them until
fully awake,** speak calmly, and do not give food or drink until they are alert.

## Causes to find and treat off-grid
- **Febrile seizure (young child with fever):** cool **gradually** — remove layers,
  lukewarm sponging — **never plunge into cold water**; treat the fever. Usually
  brief and not dangerous, but a first one should be assessed.
- **Low blood sugar** (diabetic): once fully awake and able to swallow, give sugar
  (see *Diabetic Emergencies*).
- **Low salt** from drinking only water after heavy sweating: rehydrate with oral
  rehydration solution, not plain water alone.
- **Head injury** in the last days (see *Head Injury and Concussion*).
- **Withdrawal** from alcohol or from seizure medicines that have run out — this
  causes dangerous repeated seizures; ration remaining medication carefully and
  evacuate.
- **Poisoning** or overheating (heat stroke) can also cause seizures — treat the
  cause.

**Recurrent seizures with no medical help:** protect the person during each one,
keep them safe, warm, and hydrated between, use any prescribed medication as
directed, and move toward help — repeated or prolonged seizures cause lasting harm.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
