# Seizure Response

Most seizures stop on their own within 1–3 minutes. Your job is to protect the
person from injury while it runs its course, and to recognize when it has
become an emergency.

## During the seizure
1. **Clear hard and sharp objects** away from the person.
2. **Cushion the head** with something soft — a folded jacket, your hands.
3. **Put nothing in the mouth.** They cannot swallow their tongue; forcing
   objects in breaks teeth and blocks the airway.
4. **Do not restrain** the movements. Loosen anything tight around the neck.
5. **Time the seizure** from the moment it starts.

## After the shaking stops
Roll them into the **recovery position** (on their side, head tilted slightly
back) so saliva or vomit drains. They will be confused and drowsy — this is
normal. **Stay with them until they are fully awake** and speak calmly.

## Emergency signs
Do everything you can to reach help if:
- The seizure lasts **more than 5 minutes** (status epilepticus — the seizure
  may not stop on its own and is life-threatening),
- Seizures come **back-to-back** without waking in between,
- It is the person's **first-ever seizure**,
- It happens **in water**, causes **injury**, or the person is **pregnant**,
- They do **not wake** within minutes of the shaking stopping.

## Causes to think about in a survival setting
- **High fever in children** — cool gradually (remove layers, lukewarm cloth);
  do not plunge into cold water.
- **Head injury** in the last days.
- **Drinking only water without salt** after heavy sweating — low sodium.
  Rehydrate with ORS, not plain water alone.
- **Alcohol or medication withdrawal** when supplies run out.
- **Low blood sugar** — if they wake fully, give sugar.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
