# Foraging Safety

Wild food can sustain you, but misidentification can kill in a single meal.
The rules below are absolute.

## The rules
- **Never eat anything you cannot positively identify to species.** "It looks
  like" is not identification.
- Learn a few local staples you are 100% certain of, rather than many maybes.
  Most calories come from a handful of reliable species anyway.
- Deadly lookalikes exist for most popular edibles. Two groups deserve special
  fear: **avoid all wild mushrooms unless a genuine expert identifies them**
  (deadly species mimic edible ones closely), and **avoid the carrot/parsley
  family** — poison hemlock and water hemlock kill, and they resemble wild
  carrot and parsnip.

## Warning signs to avoid
- Milky or discolored sap.
- An almond or bitter-almond smell (cyanide compounds).
- Shiny "leaves of three" (poison ivy/oak — contact poison).
- Color rules for berries ("white bad, blue safe") are **unreliable** — some
  dark berries are deadly. Identify the plant, not the color.

## The universal edibility test — last resort only
This is a multi-hour protocol for genuine starvation, not a shortcut: separate
the plant into parts; test one part at a time by holding against skin, then
lips, then tongue, then chewing without swallowing, waiting long intervals at
each stage for burning, numbness, or reaction; swallow a small amount and wait
many hours before eating more. It does not protect against all toxins —
mushrooms especially.

## Practical points
- Acorns are good food but must be leached of tannins in repeated changes of
  water until no longer bitter.
- Do not forage near roads, industry, or sprayed fields — lead and pesticides
  accumulate in plants.
- Record what you eat and when, so a reaction can be traced.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained professional care. If you
> can reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
