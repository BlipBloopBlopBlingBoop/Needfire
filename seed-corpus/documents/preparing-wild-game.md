# Preparing Wild Game

Heat spoils meat fast. Field dress promptly, keep it clean, and cook it
thoroughly — wild meat carries parasites and diseases that farm meat rarely
does.

## Field dressing
1. Bleed the animal promptly after the kill.
2. Open the belly carefully and remove the entrails **without puncturing the
   gut or bladder** — their contents contaminate meat.
3. Prop the cavity open and cool the carcass quickly, in shade, off the ground.

Skin from the legs toward the back, pulling more than cutting, keeping hair and
dirt off the meat.

## Inspect before you eat
- Check the organs. **Discard any animal with spots or nodules on the liver or
  lungs, abscesses, or that was acting sick, sluggish, or unafraid before the
  kill. Do not eat obviously diseased animals.**
- **Wear gloves or bag-covered hands when dressing game**, especially rabbits
  and rodents — tularemia and other diseases pass through cuts in skin.

## Cook it well-done
Cook all wild meat thoroughly, with no pink remaining. Bear, wild boar, and
other omnivores carry **trichinosis**; only full cooking kills it. Freezing is
not reliable for these species.

## Fish and birds
Gut fish promptly and keep them cool. Worms in fish flesh are common; thorough
cooking kills them. Birds may be plucked (keeps the fatty skin) or skinned
(faster).

## Use everything
Bones make broth; fat can be rendered for cooking and waterproofing; hides
become leather; sinew becomes cordage. After butchering, preserve what you
cannot eat soon — cool it, smoke it, or dry it, and keep raw meat away from
everything else.
