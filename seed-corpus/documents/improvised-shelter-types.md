# Improvised Shelter Types

A shelter's job is to block wind and rain and hold your body heat. **Small is
warm** — build only as big as your body, insulate underneath as much as above,
and put it up before dark and before you are exhausted.

## Choosing a site
- **Out of the wind and off wet or low ground** (cold air and water pool in
  hollows). A little above the valley floor is warmer.
- Away from hazards: dead standing trees and heavy dead branches ("widow-makers"),
  flash-flood channels, rockfall, avalanche paths, and lone trees on ridges
  (lightning).
- Near — but not on top of — firewood and water.

## Warm-weather / rain shelters
- **Tarp lean-to or A-frame.** Ridgeline between two trees; pitch one side (lean-to)
  into the wind or both sides (A-frame) for rain. Fast and effective with any
  tarp, poncho, or plastic sheet.
- **Natural lean-to.** A ridgepole against a stump or rock, ribs of branches
  angled off it, thatched with leafy boughs shingled bottom-to-top so rain sheds.

## Cold-weather shelters
- **Debris hut.** A low ridgepole and rib frame packed with **0.5 m (2 ft) of dry
  leaves/debris** all over, and the same underneath you. Fill the inside with
  leaves and burrow in — your body heat warms the small space. No fire needed.
- **Snow trench.** Dig a body-width trench in firm snow, roof it with blocks,
  branches, or a tarp. Fast, and snow is a good insulator.
- **Quinzhee.** Pile snow into a large mound, let it sinter/harden for 1–2 hours,
  then hollow it out. Poke a **ventilation hole** and keep a digging tool inside
  in case of collapse.

## Rules for any shelter
- **Insulate the floor.** Ground contact steals heat faster than cold air —
  branches, leaves, a pad, or a pack under you are essential.
- **Keep it small and seal drafts**, but always leave **ventilation**, especially
  in snow shelters or if using any flame or stove inside (carbon monoxide kills).
- Build a **debris/leaf door plug** to close the entrance.
- Work steadily and vent layers so you do not soak your clothing in sweat while
  building.

> General reference only, not professional advice. In severe conditions, shelter
> is often a higher priority than water or food — act early.
