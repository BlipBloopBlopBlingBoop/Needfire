# Poisoning First Aid

A poison can enter by being **swallowed, breathed in, absorbed through skin or
eyes, or injected** (bites and stings — see *Snakebite Treatment* and
*Anaphylaxis*). Off-grid there is no poison-control hotline and often no
hospital, so the job is to **stop more poison getting in, keep the person alive,
and give supportive care** while deciding whether to evacuate. Most people who
are breathing and conscious when you reach them will survive with good basic care.

## The universal rules
- **Make the scene safe first.** A gas, fume, or chemical that got one person
  will get you — a collapsed rescuer is just a second patient. Ventilate, cut the
  source, wear gloves, and move the person to clean air only if it is safe.
- **Do NOT make the person vomit** — for any poison. Vomiting a corrosive burns
  the throat a second time; vomiting fuel floods the lungs. Salt water, mustard,
  and finger-down-the-throat are all harmful and abandoned.
- **Do NOT try to "neutralise"** an acid with an alkali or vice versa — the
  reaction makes heat and worse injury.
- **Identify and keep the evidence:** the substance, its container and label, how
  much, and when. Keep any container, plant sample, or vomit — it guides care and
  identification.
- **Check the basics (see *Assessing a Casualty*):** if not breathing normally,
  start CPR; if unconscious but breathing, put them on their side (recovery
  position) and watch the airway; treat seizures by protecting them from injury.

## Decontamination by route
- **Swallowed:** rinse the mouth and spit. Do not give anything else by mouth
  unless the guidance below for that poison says so.
- **Skin:** remove contaminated clothing (protect your hands), **brush off dry
  powder first**, then flush with lots of clean water for 15–20 minutes (longer
  for corrosives). Do not scrub.
- **Eyes:** flush with clean water 15–20 minutes, lids held open, from the nose
  side outward; longer for alkalis. See *Eye Injuries and Chemical Splashes*.
- **Inhaled:** fresh air, rest, and oxygen if you have it.

## Managing the main poison classes

### Corrosives (drain/oven cleaner, lye, strong acid, bleach, button batteries)
They burn on the way down. If the person is **fully awake and swallowing
normally**, a few small sips of water or milk dilute it; give nothing by mouth if
they are drooling, hoarse, or struggling to swallow (airway swelling). Do not
induce vomiting; charcoal does not work. **A swallowed button/coin battery is an
emergency** — it can burn through the gullet in ~2 hours; if the person is over 1
year, awake, and can swallow, give **10 mL (2 tsp) of honey every 10 minutes**
(up to 6 doses) while moving toward help.

### Hydrocarbons (petrol, kerosene, lighter fluid, solvents, paint thinner)
The danger is aspiration into the lungs. Keep the person calm and upright, rinse
the mouth, and **do not induce vomiting** (vomiting fuel is worse than swallowing
it). Watch breathing for 24 hours — cough, fast breathing, wheeze, or blue lips
mean it reached the lungs. Charcoal does not help.

### Pesticides (insecticides, weedkillers)
Protect yourself — it is on their skin and clothing. Remove clothing and wash the
skin. **Organophosphates/carbamates** cause the body to "flood": streaming eyes
and mouth, drooling, sweating, vomiting/diarrhoea, tiny pupils, twitching, and
breathing trouble. The antidote is **atropine** (only if you have it and know the
dose); otherwise decontaminate, support the airway and breathing, and evacuate.

### Medicines and drugs
Find the packet: name, strength, how many, when.
- **Paracetamol / acetaminophen** — few symptoms early, then **liver failure over
  2–4 days**. The antidote works best within 8 hours and you likely lack it —
  evacuate. Overdose is roughly ≥150 mg/kg or a large handful.
- **Aspirin** — ringing ears, fast deep breathing, vomiting, confusion.
- **Opioids/sedatives** — pinpoint pupils and slow/absent breathing. **Breathing
  is the killer:** give rescue breaths, and **naloxone** if you have it (into nose
  or muscle, repeat every 2–3 min; it wears off in 30–90 min, so keep watching).
- **Alcohol** — recovery position, keep warm, never leave alone; watch for slow
  breathing, vomiting, cold, and low blood sugar. **Methanol/antifreeze** (some
  homebrew, windshield fluid) causes blindness and death — evacuate.

### Plants, berries, and mushrooms
Keep a sample. Support and hydrate. **With mushrooms, late symptoms are the
dangerous ones:** the deadliest species (Amanita) cause vomiting/diarrhoea
**6–24 hours** later, then a false recovery, then fatal liver failure — never be
reassured by a slow start; evacuate. Note who else may have shared the food.

### Gases — carbon monoxide, the shelter killer
CO from stoves, heaters, engines, generators, or charcoal in an enclosed space is
**invisible and odourless**: headache, nausea, dizziness, confusion. **A headache
shared by everyone in the shelter is CO until proven otherwise** — get everyone
out, ventilate, and give oxygen; severe cases need hours of it. Fire smoke can
also carry cyanide. See *Carbon Monoxide — The Silent Killer*.

### Botulism (badly preserved food)
Blurred/double vision, drooping eyelids, dry mouth, then **descending weakness**
and breathing failure, hours to days after eating. Discard bulging, foul, or
suspect cans and ferments (see *Food Safety and Preservation*). Support breathing
and evacuate — it needs antitoxin.

## Activated charcoal
If you carry it, it binds many drugs and plant toxins **given within about an
hour** of swallowing. Dose ~**1 g/kg** (about 25–50 g for an adult) in water,
**only** if the person is fully alert with a protected airway. It does **NOT**
bind alcohols, fuels/solvents, corrosives, or metals (iron, lithium) — and must
never be given to a drowsy person (they can inhale it).

## Ongoing care and when to evacuate
Reassess every few minutes: response, breathing, colour, and any new drowsiness,
vomiting, seizures, or breathing trouble. Keep the airway open, keep them warm,
and give small sips of water if fully alert.

**Move toward help early — before a crisis — for:** paracetamol, methanol/
antifreeze, organophosphate pesticide, a swallowed button battery, any corrosive,
a deadly mushroom, or an opioid you cannot keep reversed. **Trouble breathing,
airway swelling (drooling, voice change, noisy breathing), repeated seizures, or a
falling level of consciousness mean evacuate now** if at all possible.

> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
