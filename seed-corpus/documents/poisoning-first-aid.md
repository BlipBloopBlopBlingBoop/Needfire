# Poisoning First Aid

**Do NOT induce vomiting** for any poison. Vomiting corrosives burns the throat
twice; vomiting fuels floods the lungs. First, identify what happened: the
substance, the container and its label, how much, and when. Keep the container.

## By route
- **Swallowed**: rinse the mouth with water and spit. Give **nothing further by
  mouth** — no milk, no salt water, no charcoal — unless a trusted reference
  for that specific substance says otherwise.
- **On skin**: remove contaminated clothing (protect your own hands) and
  **flush the skin with water for 15–20 minutes**.
- **In the eyes**: **flush for 15–20 minutes** with lukewarm clean water,
  eyelids held open, flowing from the nose side outward.
- **Inhaled**: get the person to **fresh air**. **Protect yourself first** —
  do not become the second casualty in a fume-filled space.

## Carbon monoxide — the shelter killer
CO from stoves, heaters, engines, and charcoal in an enclosed shelter is
**invisible and odorless**. It causes headache, nausea, dizziness, and
confusion. **A headache shared by everyone in the shelter is CO until proven
otherwise.** Get everyone out into fresh air and ventilate before returning.

## If unconscious
If unconscious but breathing, place them in the **recovery position** and
monitor the airway. If not breathing normally, start CPR.

## Poisons to know in a survival setting
- **Carbon monoxide** (above) — never burn anything in an unventilated space.
- **Wild mushrooms**: the deadliest species cause symptoms that are **delayed
  6+ hours** — late onset is a bad sign, not a reassuring one.
- **Botulism** from badly canned food: blurred vision, drooping lids,
  descending weakness. Discard bulging or foul cans.
- **Fuel siphoning**: even a small aspirated mouthful can inflame the lungs.
  Do not induce vomiting; watch breathing for 24 hours.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
