# Shock — Recognition and First Aid

**Shock** here means circulatory shock: the body is no longer moving enough
oxygen-carrying blood to its organs. It is a medical emergency that follows
serious bleeding, major burns, severe dehydration or diarrhoea, a crush injury,
a severe allergic reaction, or a heart problem. Untreated, it kills. This page
is about that whole-body collapse — **not** the electric shock of a live wire.

## Recognizing shock
The picture builds over minutes to hours:
- **Skin: pale, cold, clammy, sweaty** (in some causes it may instead be warm
  and flushed early — treat anyway).
- **Pulse: fast and weak** ("thready").
- **Breathing: fast and shallow.**
- **Mind: anxious or restless, then confused, drowsy, and finally unresponsive.**
- Thirst, nausea, weakness, and a grey or blue tinge to lips and nailbeds.

Assume shock after any major injury even before these signs appear — it is far
easier to prevent than to reverse.

## First aid
1. **Treat the cause first.** Stop severe bleeding with direct pressure; give
   adrenaline for anaphylaxis; cool a burn. Shock will not lift while the cause
   continues.
2. **Lay them flat** and keep them still. If there is no head, chest, spine, or
   leg fracture and it does not worsen breathing, **raise the legs about 30 cm**
   to return blood to the core.
3. **Keep them warm.** Put insulation under and over them; cold deepens shock.
   Do not overheat.
4. **Nothing by mouth** — no food, water, or drink, even if they beg. It risks
   choking if they lose consciousness or need surgery. If they are fully alert
   and help is many hours away and the cause is fluid loss (diarrhoea, heat),
   small sips of oral rehydration solution may be the only option — judge the
   choking risk.
5. **Reassure and monitor** breathing and responsiveness constantly. If they
   stop breathing normally, start CPR.
6. **Get advanced help as fast as possible** — shock needs fluids or blood and
   definitive care you cannot give in the field.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
