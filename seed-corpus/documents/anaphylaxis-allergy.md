# Anaphylaxis and Severe Allergic Reactions

Anaphylaxis is a whole-body allergic reaction that can kill within minutes by
closing the airway or crashing blood pressure. It is one of the few emergencies
where a single treatment — **adrenaline** — is dramatically life-saving, which is
why anyone with a known severe allergy should carry two auto-injectors, and why a
Bothy medical kit should stock adrenaline if at all possible.

## Recognise it fast
Suspect anaphylaxis when, **soon after** a sting, food, or medicine, a person
develops any combination of:
- Swelling of the **lips, tongue, or throat**; a hoarse voice; a lump-in-throat
  feeling.
- **Trouble breathing, wheeze, or persistent cough.**
- Widespread **hives, flushing, or itching**.
- **Vomiting, cramps, or diarrhoea.**
- **Dizziness, collapse, or a sense of doom** (falling blood pressure).
Rapid onset after exposure is the key clue. Any breathing difficulty, throat/mouth
swelling, or faintness means a life-threatening reaction — treat now, don't wait.

## Adrenaline (epinephrine) first — and fast
1. **Give adrenaline immediately** into the muscle of the **outer mid-thigh**,
   through clothing if needed. Hold it there several seconds, then rub the spot.
   - **Adult: 0.3 mg** (auto-injector, or 0.3 mL of 1 mg/mL "1:1000" solution).
   - **Child: 0.15 mg** (roughly under 25–30 kg / 55–66 lb).
2. **Repeat after 5–15 minutes** if the person is not clearly improving or is
   getting worse. A second dose is common and expected.
3. **Antihistamines are NOT a substitute** — they ease itching and hives only, and
   do nothing for the airway or blood pressure. Never delay adrenaline to try them.

Adrenaline given early is very safe; the dangerous mistake is giving it late or
not at all. **When in doubt, give it.**

## Off-grid: no adrenaline available
There is no true substitute, but you can still save a life with airway care and
positioning while you get help or an injector:
- **Position correctly** (below) — this alone keeps blood pressure and the airway
  working.
- If there is an **asthma-type wheeze and a reliever inhaler**, give it (up to 10
  puffs) — it can open the airways a little.
- **Antihistamines**, if you have them, help the skin symptoms only.
- Be ready to open the airway and give **rescue breaths / CPR** if breathing
  stops.
- **Send for help and adrenaline the instant symptoms appear** — do not wait to
  see how bad it gets. Adrenaline anywhere within reach is worth going for.

## Position — done wrong, it kills
- **Faint / low blood pressure:** lie flat and **raise the legs**.
- **Breathing is hard:** let them **sit up**, but do not let them stand.
- **Vomiting or unconscious but breathing:** **recovery position** (on the side).
- **Visibly pregnant:** lie on her **left side**.
- **Never stand or walk the person, or sit them up suddenly** — a sudden upright
  move when blood pressure is low can stop the heart.

## Afterward
Even after full recovery, symptoms can return **hours later** with no new exposure
(a **biphasic reaction**). Watch closely for **at least 4–6 hours**, longer after
a severe attack, and keep a second dose within reach. Remove a bee stinger by
**scraping it out sideways** (pinching squeezes in more venom). **Evacuate to
medical care even if they seem fully recovered** — especially if adrenaline was
needed or was unavailable. Afterward, identify and avoid the trigger, and get
injectors for anyone at risk.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
