# Anaphylaxis and Severe Allergic Reactions

Anaphylaxis is a whole-body allergic reaction that can kill within minutes.
Suspect it when, soon after a sting, food, or medicine, a person develops any
combination of: swelling of the lips, tongue, or throat; hoarse voice or
wheeze; widespread hives or flushing; vomiting or cramps; dizziness or
collapse. Rapid onset after exposure is the key clue.

## Epinephrine first — and fast
1. **Give epinephrine (adrenaline) immediately** into the muscle of the
   **outer mid-thigh**, through clothing if needed. Auto-injector doses:
   **adult 0.3 mg**, **child 0.15 mg** (roughly under 25–30 kg / 55–66 lb).
2. **Repeat after 5–15 minutes** if the person is not clearly improving.
3. **Antihistamines are NOT a substitute** for epinephrine. They may ease
   itching but do nothing for the airway or blood pressure. Never delay
   epinephrine to try them.

Epinephrine given early is very safe; the dangerous mistake is giving it late.

## Position
- **Lie them flat and raise the legs.**
- If breathing is hard, let them **sit up**.
- If vomiting or unconscious but breathing, use the **recovery position**.
- A visibly **pregnant** person lies on her **left side**.
- **Do NOT stand the person up suddenly** — a sudden upright move can cause
  fatal collapse when blood pressure is low.

## Afterward
Symptoms can return **hours later** with no new exposure (a biphasic
reaction). **Watch the person closely for at least 4–6 hours**, longer after a
severe reaction, and keep a second epinephrine dose within reach. Remove any
remaining stinger by scraping it out. Evacuate to medical care even if they
recover fully.

> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
