# Seed Saving and Basic Agriculture

Saving seed closes the loop on food production: one harvest becomes the next
planting, indefinitely. It is essential for long-term self-sufficiency.

## Use open-pollinated, not hybrid
- **Open-pollinated (including heirloom)** varieties breed true: their saved seed
  grows into the same plant. These are what you save.
- **Hybrid (F1)** seed does not breed true — the next generation is unpredictable.
  Productive for one season, but not for seed saving.

## Easiest crops to start with
Self-pollinating annuals give reliable seed with little isolation: **beans, peas,
tomatoes, lettuce, peppers**. Save from your healthiest, most productive plants.

## How to save
1. Let the fruit/pod fully mature on the plant — past eating ripeness for seed.
2. **Dry-seeded** crops (beans, peas, lettuce, grains): let pods/heads dry on the
   plant, then thresh and winnow.
3. **Wet-seeded** crops (tomato, cucumber): scoop seeds, ferment the pulp in water
   2–3 days to remove the germination-inhibiting coating, rinse, and dry.
4. Dry seeds thoroughly, then store cool, dark, and dry in airtight containers. A
   common rule: storage temperature (°F) + humidity (%) should total under 100.
   Label with variety and year.

## Beyond seed
- Rotate crop families between beds to reduce pests and soil depletion.
- Build soil with compost and cover crops (legumes fix nitrogen).
- Keep a portion of seed in reserve every year as insurance against a failed crop.

> Most seed stays viable for several years if stored well; viability declines with
> time, so refresh your stock by growing it out periodically.
