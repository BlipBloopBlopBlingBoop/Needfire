# Drowning Response

Drowning is **silent and fast** — not the splashing and shouting of films. A
drowning person slips under with little noise, often within a minute. Watch
swimmers, and count heads often.

## Rescue: reach, throw, row — don't go
Rescue from shore first. **Reach** with a stick, paddle, or clothing; **throw**
a rope or anything that floats; **row** a boat to them. Enter the water only
as a last resort and bring something that floats — **a drowning person will
climb on you and pull you under.**

## Once out of the water
1. **Check breathing** for no more than 10 seconds. Gasping is not breathing.
2. If not breathing normally, **give 5 initial rescue breaths BEFORE starting
   compressions** (tilt head, lift chin, seal your mouth over theirs — mouth
   and nose for a small child). Drowning arrest comes from **lack of oxygen**,
   so ventilation comes first — the opposite of typical adult cardiac arrest.
3. Then continue CPR at **30 compressions to 2 breaths**.
4. **Expect vomiting** — it happens in most drowning resuscitations. **Roll
   them to the side, clear the mouth, and continue.**
5. **Do NOT waste time trying to "drain water"** from the lungs with abdominal
   thrusts or by hanging them head-down. It does not work and delays CPR.

## Hypothermia usually comes too
Cold water victims are often hypothermic. **Handle gently**, remove wet
clothing, insulate from the ground, and warm gradually. Do not give up
resuscitation early on a cold victim — **"not dead until warm and dead."**

## Watch for 24 hours
Anyone who inhaled water can deteriorate over the next 24 hours — worsening
cough, fast or laboured breathing, unusual tiredness. **Watch them**, and
treat new breathing trouble as an emergency.
