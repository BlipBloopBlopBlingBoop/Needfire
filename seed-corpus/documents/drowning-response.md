# Drowning Response

Drowning is **silent and fast** — not the splashing and shouting of films. A
drowning person slips under with little noise, often within a minute. Watch
swimmers and count heads often. The arrest is from **lack of oxygen**, so with
drowning, breaths come first.

## Rescue: reach, throw, row — don't go
Rescue from shore whenever you can. **Reach** with a stick, paddle, pole, or
clothing; **throw** a rope or anything that floats; **row** a boat out. Enter the
water only as a last resort, and take something that floats to keep between you and
them. **A drowning person will climb on you and push you under** — more rescuers
drown than victims. Do not become the second casualty.

## Getting them out
Keep them **horizontal** as you bring them out and lay them on their back — hauling
a near-drowning person upright can crash their blood pressure. If they dived or
fell and a **neck injury** is possible, keep the head and neck aligned as you move
them — but an open airway and breathing **always come first**; don't let spinal
caution stop you resuscitating.

## Once out of the water
1. **Check breathing** for no more than 10 seconds. Gasping is not breathing.
2. **Breathing normally:** put them on their **side** (recovery position), keep
   them warm, and watch continuously — breathing can fail again.
3. **Not breathing normally:** give **5 initial rescue breaths BEFORE
   compressions** (tilt head, lift chin, seal your mouth over theirs — mouth and
   nose for a small child), then continue CPR at **30 compressions to 2 breaths**.
   Keep the breaths in — do **not** switch to hands-only for a drowning victim.
4. **Expect vomiting** — it happens in most drowning resuscitations. Roll them to
   the side, sweep the mouth clear, roll back, and continue.
5. **Do NOT try to "drain the lungs"** by pressing the belly or hanging them
   upside down — it does not work and delays the CPR that saves them.

## Cold water can save them
Cold-water victims are usually **hypothermic**, and the cold protects the brain.
Handle very gently (a cold heart is easily upset), remove wet clothing, insulate
above and below, and warm gradually. **Keep resuscitating far longer than usual —
"not dead until warm and dead."** People have recovered fully after long
submersion in cold water.

## Watch for 24 hours
Anyone who inhaled water can deteriorate hours later ("secondary" or "dry"
drowning): worsening cough, fast or laboured breathing, chest tightness, foaming,
or unusual sleepiness. **Watch them,** keep them upright and calm, and treat new
breathing trouble as an emergency — evacuate.
