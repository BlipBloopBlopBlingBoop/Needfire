# Levers, Pulleys, and Mechanical Advantage

Mechanical advantage lets one person move loads far beyond their strength — lift a
vehicle, drag a log, free a stuck cart, or raise a ridgepole. The rule is simple:
**you trade distance for force.** Move the effort a long way to move the load a
short way, and the force multiplies.

## Levers
A rigid bar pivoting on a **fulcrum**. Force multiplies by the ratio of the two
arm lengths:

    load force = effort × (effort arm ÷ load arm)

Put the fulcrum **close to the load** and push at the far end of a long lever. A
long, strong pole and a solid fulcrum (rock, log) will lift or shift enormous
weights. This is also how a **wheelbarrow, pry bar, oars, and shovels** work.

## Pulleys and block-and-tackle
- A **single fixed pulley** just changes direction (MA = 1) — useful to pull down
  instead of up.
- Mechanical advantage comes from **rope segments that actually support the moving
  load.** Count them: the **ideal MA ≈ the number of rope parts pulling on the
  moving block.** A two-sheave tackle rigged to 4 supporting parts gives ~4:1 —
  100 kg needs ~25 kg of pull.
- **Friction is real:** each pulley/sheave loses roughly 10% of the force. A "4:1"
  in practice may deliver ~3:1, more so with makeshift rollers or carabiners as
  pulleys. Use real pulleys where you can.
- You pull **as many times the distance** as the MA: a 4:1 needs 4 m of rope
  pulled to raise the load 1 m.

## Safe working load — do not guess
A rope or anchor near its breaking strength **will fail**, often under a sudden
shock. Use a **safety factor**:
- **Life-loaded systems** (a person's weight, rescue): keep the load at **1/10 or
  less** of the rope/hardware breaking strength.
- **Gear only**, no one under or near it: **1/5** is a common minimum.
- **Mechanical advantage multiplies the force on the anchor and rope**, so a 4:1
  hauling 100 kg can put ~400 kg-plus into the anchor — build anchors for the
  multiplied load, not the load you see.

## Practical hauling
- Combine methods: a lever to break a load free, then a tackle to move it.
- Keep everyone **out of the line of a loaded rope** — a snapped rope or slipping
  load whips back lethally.
- Use a **progress-capture** (a friction hitch or a jammed knot) so a slip does not
  lose all your gain, and take up slack in stages.

## Safety
Every hauling system stores energy that releases violently if it fails. Anchor to
something that will truly hold, inspect ropes for damage, back up critical anchors,
and never stand where a break would strike you.
