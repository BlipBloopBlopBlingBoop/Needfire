# Finding Direction by Sun and Stars

When you have no compass, the sun by day and the stars by night still tell you
direction. Confirm with more than one method and note landmarks as you go (see
*Navigation with Map and Compass*).

## By the sun (day)
- **Rise and set:** the sun rises in the **east** and sets in the **west** (exactly
  so at the equinoxes; north of due east/west in northern summer, south of it in
  winter).
- **Solar noon:** at midday the sun is due **south** in the Northern Hemisphere
  and due **north** in the Southern; a vertical stick's shadow is shortest and
  points to that meridian.
- **Shadow-stick method:** push a straight stick upright in level ground. Mark the
  shadow tip, wait 15–30 minutes, mark again. The line from the **first** mark to
  the second runs roughly **west→east** (first mark is west); a line square to it
  is north–south.

## By the stars (night)
- **Northern Hemisphere — Polaris (the North Star):** find the Big Dipper (Plough);
  the two stars at the end of its "cup" are the **pointers** — follow them about
  five times their own spacing to Polaris. Polaris sits over **true north**, and
  its height above the horizon roughly equals your **latitude**. Cassiopeia (a "W")
  sits opposite the Dipper across Polaris and helps when the Dipper is low.
- **Southern Hemisphere — the Southern Cross:** identify the true Cross (with its
  two bright pointer stars nearby). Extend the long axis of the Cross about **4.5
  times** its length to an empty point in the sky; straight down from there to the
  horizon is roughly **south**.
- **Any star's drift:** watch a star against a fixed foresight (two stakes). Rising
  = you are facing roughly east; falling = west; drifting right = south; drifting
  left = north (Northern Hemisphere).

## The moon
A rough dusk/dawn aid: if the moon rises **before sunset**, its illuminated side
faces roughly **west**; if it rises **after midnight**, the lit side faces roughly
**east** (the sun is always what lights it).

## Cautions
Celestial direction gives you a **bearing, not a fix** — it tells you which way is
north, not where you are. Combine it with terrain, your back-trail, and daylight
planning, and do not travel blind at night over dangerous ground.

> General reference only. Practice these before you need them; a real compass and
> map remain far more reliable.
