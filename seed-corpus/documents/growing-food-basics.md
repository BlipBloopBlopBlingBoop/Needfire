# Growing Food — Garden Basics

A garden turns sun, water, and soil into a renewing food supply. It takes weeks to
months to yield, so **start before you are desperate**. Pair this with *Seed
Saving and Basic Agriculture* and *Composting and Soil*.

## What plants need
- **Sun:** most vegetables want **6+ hours of direct sun**. Leafy greens tolerate
  partial shade; fruiting crops (tomatoes, squash, beans, peppers) need full sun.
- **Water:** steady moisture, especially while germinating and fruiting. Deep,
  less-frequent watering grows deeper roots than daily sprinkles. **Mulch** (straw,
  leaves) holds moisture and suppresses weeds.
- **Soil:** loose, fertile, draining soil. Feed it with compost and manure (see
  *Composting and Soil*).
- **Warmth and season:** know your frost dates. Cool-season crops (peas, greens,
  roots) take light frost; warm-season crops die at frost — start them after the
  last frost or indoors early.

## High-value crops for calories and nutrition
- **Calories/storage:** potatoes, sweet potatoes, winter squash, beans, maize,
  grains.
- **Fast greens & vitamins:** lettuce, kale, chard, spinach, radish — quick and
  rich in the vitamins stored food lacks.
- **Productive per space:** beans (also fix nitrogen), tomatoes, courgette/squash,
  cucumbers.
- **Perennials** (fruit trees, berries, rhubarb, asparagus) yield year after year
  once established — plant them early.

## Planting
1. **Prepare the bed:** loosen soil, remove weeds, work in compost.
2. **Sow at the right depth** — a rough rule is **2–3× the seed's diameter**; tiny
   seeds barely covered, big seeds (beans) deeper. Keep seed moist until it sprouts.
3. **Space to the mature size**, not the seedling — crowded plants compete and
   yield little. Thin seedlings ruthlessly.
4. Sow in **succession** (a little every few weeks) for a continuous harvest rather
   than one glut.

## Keeping it productive
- **Rotate crops** — don't grow the same family in the same spot each year; it
  drains specific nutrients and builds up pests and disease.
- **Companion planting and diversity** reduce pest wipeouts; a monoculture is
  fragile.
- **Save seed** from your best open-pollinated plants for next season (see *Seed
  Saving*).
- **Protect from pests** with barriers, hand-picking, and encouraging predators
  before reaching for anything harsher.

> General reference only. Crops, timing, and pests vary enormously by climate and
> region — learn what grows locally and keep notes each season.
