# CPR for Adults, Children, and Infants

If a person is unresponsive and not breathing normally (gasping counts as not
breathing normally), their heart has likely stopped. CPR keeps blood moving to
the brain. Start immediately — every minute without it lowers survival.

## Steps
1. **Check response**: shout, tap the shoulders. Look for normal breathing for
   no more than 10 seconds.
2. **Call for help**. Send someone for aid and for an AED (defibrillator) if one
   could exist nearby. If alone with an adult, get help first; if alone with a
   child or infant, **do CPR for about 2 minutes before leaving them**.
3. **Compress the centre of the chest**, hard and fast:
   - Rate **100–120 per minute**, on a firm surface.
   - Adult: two hands, at least **5 cm (2 in)** deep (no more than 6 cm / 2.4 in).
   - Child: one or two hands, about **5 cm (2 in)**.
   - Infant: **two fingers** (or two thumbs, hands encircling the chest), about
     **4 cm (1.5 in)**.
   - Let the chest **recoil fully** between compressions; minimise pauses.
4. **If trained in rescue breaths**, give **30 compressions then 2 breaths**
   (tilt head, lift chin; cover an infant's mouth and nose). If not trained or
   unwilling, continuous hands-only compressions are effective for adults.
   Breaths matter more for children, infants, and drowning victims.

## AED
Attach an AED **as soon as it arrives**, turn it on, and follow its voice
prompts. Resume compressions immediately after any shock.

## Do not stop
Continue until the person shows signs of life, trained help takes over, an AED
tells you to stand clear, or you are physically unable to continue. Swap rescuers
every 2 minutes if possible.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
