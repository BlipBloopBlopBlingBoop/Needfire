# CPR for Adults, Children, and Infants

If a person is unresponsive and not breathing normally (gasping counts as **not**
breathing normally), their heart has likely stopped. CPR pushes blood to the
brain and buys time. **Start immediately** — every minute without it drops the
chance of survival. It is far better to do imperfect CPR than none.

## 1. Check and call
1. **Check response:** shout and tap the shoulders. Look for normal breathing for
   **no more than 10 seconds** — a few irregular gasps are not breathing.
2. **Send for help and an AED** (defibrillator) if one could exist nearby. If you
   are **alone with an adult**, get help/AED first; if **alone with a child or
   infant**, do **2 minutes of CPR before leaving** to get help.

## 2. Compressions — hard, fast, and deep
On a firm surface, in the **centre of the chest**:
- **Rate 100–120 per minute** (the beat of "Stayin' Alive"; the toolkit has a CPR
  metronome).
- **Adult:** two hands, at least **5 cm (2 in)** deep, no more than 6 cm.
- **Child:** one or two hands, about **5 cm (2 in)** (about ⅓ chest depth).
- **Infant (under 1 yr):** **two fingers**, or two thumbs with hands encircling
  the chest, about **4 cm (1.5 in)** (about ⅓ chest depth).
- **Let the chest recoil fully** between compressions and **minimise pauses** —
  interruptions cost lives.

## 3. Rescue breaths
If trained and willing, give **30 compressions then 2 breaths** (tilt the head,
lift the chin, pinch the nose; for an infant seal your mouth over mouth-and-nose).
Each breath is a gentle 1 second, just enough to make the chest rise. If untrained
or unwilling, **continuous hands-only compressions** are effective for adults.
**Breaths matter more** for children, infants, and drowning, poisoning, or
choking victims — their arrest is from lack of oxygen, so include the breaths.

## 4. AED (if you have one)
Attach it **as soon as it arrives**, turn it on, and follow its voice prompts —
it will not shock unless a shock is needed. Resume compressions immediately after
any shock or "no shock advised." **Swap rescuers every 2 minutes** to keep
compressions strong (CPR is exhausting).

## Off-grid: when is it reasonable to stop?
Continue until the person shows **signs of life** (movement, breathing, coughing),
an AED tells you to stand clear, someone can take over, or you cannot physically
continue.
- **Without a defibrillator or advanced care, survival is very low** once there
  are **no signs of life after 20–30 minutes** of good CPR. If you are exhausted,
  alone, in danger, or help will genuinely never come, it is acceptable — and not
  a failure — to stop.
- **Keep going far longer** if the person is **cold/hypothermic, was pulled from
  cold water, was buried in an avalanche, or was struck by lightning** — the cold
  protects the brain and full recovery is possible after prolonged CPR. The saying
  is **"not dead until warm and dead."**
- A person who survives will need close monitoring and evacuation — cardiac arrest
  can recur.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
