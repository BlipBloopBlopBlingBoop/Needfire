# Cooking Without Power

Cooking makes food safe and digestible and stretches what you have. Off-grid it
also means fire, fuel, and fumes — plan for all three.

## Ventilation first
**Never run a charcoal grill, camp stove, or any combustion appliance indoors**
— carbon monoxide is invisible and deadly (see *Carbon Monoxide — The Silent
Killer*). Cook outdoors or in a well-ventilated space with the flame vented out.

## Methods
- **Open fire / coals.** Cook over a bed of glowing coals, not leaping flames —
  coals give steady, controllable heat. A grate or flat stones hold pots.
- **Rocket stove.** An L-shaped insulated burn chamber concentrates a small
  amount of wood into a hot, efficient flame — far less fuel than an open fire.
- **Earth oven / pit.** Heat rocks in a pit, add wrapped food and cover with
  earth for slow, fuel-free baking and roasting.
- **Solar cooker.** A reflective box or panel cooks slowly on sunny days with no
  fuel at all — good for stews and grains, useless at night or in cloud.
- **Retained-heat (hay box).** Bring a pot to a boil, then bury it in heavy
  insulation; it finishes cooking on stored heat, saving fuel for grains, beans,
  and stews.

## Safe cooking temperatures
Heat kills the pathogens that cause food poisoning. Cook thoroughly, especially
meat:
- **Poultry:** cooked through, juices run clear, no pink at the bone (~74 °C /
  165 °F).
- **Ground/minced meat:** cooked through, no pink centre (~71 °C / 160 °F).
- **Whole cuts of red meat:** at least ~63 °C (145 °F) then a short rest.
- **Reheat leftovers** to steaming hot all the way through.
Without a thermometer, cook until juices run clear and there is no raw-textured
centre. When unsure, cook it longer.

## Fuel and safety
- Use **dry, seasoned hardwood**; green or resinous wood smokes and coats pots.
- Keep raw and cooked food and utensils **separate** to avoid cross-contamination.
- Keep water and a means to smother flames within reach; never leave a cooking
  fire unattended. Build fires on bare earth or stone, clear of overhanging fuel.

> General reference only, not professional advice. When in doubt about whether
> food is cooked or safe, cook it more or throw it out.
