# Knots and Cordage

Six knots cover most needs. Practice them until your hands know them.

## The six
- **Square knot** — joins two lines of the same size. Right over left, then
  left over right; both ends exit on the same side. For bundles and bandages,
  **not for load-bearing** — it can capsize.
- **Sheet bend** — joins lines of different sizes. Make a bight in the thicker
  line; pass the thinner up through it, around behind both parts, and back
  under itself.
- **Bowline** — a fixed loop that will not slip: the rescue loop. Make a small
  loop in the standing line; the end comes up through it, around behind the
  standing part, and back down through the small loop.
- **Clove hitch** — fast, adjustable attachment to a post: two turns around
  the post, the second crossing the first, end tucked under the last turn.
- **Taut-line hitch** — adjustable tension for guy lines: two turns inside the
  loop around the standing part, then one more outside; slides to tighten,
  grips under load.
- **Trucker's hitch** — mechanical advantage for cinching: make a loop midline,
  pass the end around the anchor and back through the loop, haul tight, tie
  off with two half hitches.

## Rope care
Wet, frozen, or sandy rope weakens; sharp edges cut loaded rope instantly —
pad them. Inspect for fraying. **When cord is life-safety (climbing, hauling a
person), do not improvise — use proper rope and knots you have practiced.**

## Making cordage
Twist plant fiber (nettle, inner bark, dry grass) with the **reverse-wrap
two-ply** method: twist each bundle away from you, wrap them around each other
toward you, splicing in fiber as ends thin. Paracord inner strands serve for
line and thread.

## Lashings
For shelter frames, the **square lashing** binds poles at right angles: wrap
alternately over and under both poles several times, then frap (wrap between
the poles) to tighten, and tie off.
