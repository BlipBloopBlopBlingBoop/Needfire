# Diabetic Emergencies — Low and High Blood Sugar

People with diabetes can crash in two opposite directions. **Low blood sugar
(hypoglycaemia) is the immediate killer** and comes on in minutes; high blood
sugar (hyperglycaemia) builds over hours to days. When in doubt and the person
is conscious and can swallow, **give sugar** — it rescues a low fast and does
little harm to a high in the short term.

## Low blood sugar (hypoglycaemia) — the fast emergency
Triggers: too much insulin, a missed meal, or hard exertion.
Signs come on **quickly**: shaky, sweaty, pale, hungry, irritable or confused,
weak, trembling, slurred speech, then collapse or seizure.

**If conscious and able to swallow:**
1. Give **fast sugar now** — about 15–20 g: a glass of fruit juice or sugary
   (non-diet) drink, several glucose tablets, a spoon of sugar or honey.
2. **Wait 10–15 minutes.** If no better, repeat the sugar.
3. Once they improve, give a **longer-lasting snack** (bread, crackers) to hold
   the level.

**If unconscious or unable to swallow safely:**
- **Do not put food or drink in their mouth** — choking risk.
- Place them in the **recovery position** and get emergency help. If they have a
  glucagon kit and someone trained, use it. Treat a seizure by protecting them
  from injury.

## High blood sugar (hyperglycaemia) — the slow emergency
Triggers: missed insulin, illness, infection.
Signs build **slowly over hours/days**: very thirsty, frequent urination, dry
skin, tiredness, nausea, deep sighing breathing, and a **fruity/acetone smell**
on the breath (diabetic ketoacidosis — dangerous).
- The person needs **insulin and medical care**, which you usually cannot give
  in the field — **get advanced help.**
- Encourage **sips of water** if fully alert to fight dehydration.

## When you cannot tell which it is
If the person is conscious and can swallow, **treat as a low: give sugar.** A low
kills in minutes; a brief extra dose of sugar will not meaningfully worsen a
high. If they do not improve within 15 minutes, get help urgently.

> General reference only, not a substitute for trained medical care. If you can
> reach professional help or a trained responder, do so.
