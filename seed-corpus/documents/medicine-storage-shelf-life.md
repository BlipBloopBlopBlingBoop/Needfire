# Storing Medicines and Shelf Life

Medicines kept well stay effective for a long time; kept badly they degrade fast.
Understanding storage and expiry lets you build and manage a stock sensibly.

## Store cool, dry, and dark
Heat, humidity, and light are what break drugs down.
- **Cool:** most medicines want a stable room temperature; heat accelerates decay.
  The bathroom and a hot vehicle are the *worst* places (heat + damp).
- **Dry:** keep the desiccant packet in tablet bottles; humidity ruins tablets and
  capsules. Keep lids tight.
- **Dark:** light-sensitive drugs come in amber or opaque containers — keep them so.
- Keep medicines in **original packaging** with their labels, dates, and dosing.
- **Store all medicines out of reach of children** — locked if possible.

## Expiry dates and potency
- The expiry date is the manufacturer's guarantee of **full potency** to that
  date, not a poison date. Many stable **solid** drugs (many tablets/capsules)
  retain most of their strength for years beyond it if stored well — they mostly
  lose strength gradually rather than becoming toxic.
- **Important exceptions — do NOT rely on past expiry:** liquids and suspensions,
  reconstituted antibiotics, insulin and other biologics, nitroglycerin, eye
  drops, EpiPens/adrenaline, and anything that has changed **colour, smell,
  texture, or clarity**. Tetracycline is a classic drug not to use degraded.
- **Life-critical medicines** (adrenaline, insulin, heart drugs, seizure meds)
  should be kept in date and rotated — do not gamble potency where a weak dose
  could kill.

## The cold chain
Some medicines (insulin, many vaccines, some antibiotics) must stay **refrigerated
but not frozen**. Off-grid, use an evaporative "pot-in-pot" cooler, a spring/well,
a shaded buried cache, or a battery/solar fridge; **freezing destroys insulin and
many vaccines** just as heat does.

## Managing a stock
- **Rotate: first in, first out.** Use the oldest first and restock behind it.
- Keep a written **inventory with dates** and check it periodically.
- Dispose of clearly spoiled medicines safely; do not contaminate water sources.

> Educational reference only, not medical advice. Do not use degraded or
> life-critical medicines past their date when trained care or fresh stock is
> reachable.
